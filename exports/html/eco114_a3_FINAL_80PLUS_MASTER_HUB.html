A3 Format and Negative Marking Strategy

Section A: True/False

Negative marking: correct answers earn marks, wrong answers lose marks. Answer only when you can state the correction rule in one sentence. Leave blank when guessing.

Section B: MCQ

Eliminate options by model first: price-setting or price-taking, private or social, movement or shift, Nash or Pareto. Do not pick from vibes — pick because the graph/formula confirms it.

Section C

Show calculations. Label axes. State formula before substituting. Marks come from: axes, curves, equilibrium, policy/social outcome, shaded welfare area, interpretation.

Decision rules
  • Answer T/F if you can identify the exact trap.
  • Skip when all you have is a vibe and negative marking applies.
  • For MCQs: eliminate options that use the wrong model.
  • For graphs: write the condition next to the graph: MR=MC, Qd=Qs, MSC=MPC+MEC, MRS=MRT.
  • For calculations: write units and use post-policy quantities after taxes or controls.

Markets — Unit 8: Competitive Markets, Taxes and Price Controls

Unit 8: Competitive Markets

Core story

Unit 8 models markets with many buyers and sellers where no single participant sets price. Demand represents willingness to pay (slopes down). Supply represents willingness to accept/MC (slopes up). Competitive equilibrium: Qd=Qs — market clears. The individual price-taking firm faces horizontal demand at market price and chooses q where P=MC.

Policy: a price ceiling (max price) binds only below equilibrium → creates shortage (Qd>Qs). A price floor (min price) binds only above equilibrium → creates surplus (Qs>Qd). A per-unit tax creates a wedge between buyer price and seller price; post-tax quantity falls; tax revenue = tax × Qt; DWL = ½ × tax × (Q* – Qt).

Key definitions
TermPlainExam useTrap
Consumer surplus (CS)Buyer gains from paying less than WTP.Area below demand and above price.Do not put CS below supply.
Producer surplus (PS)Seller gains above WTA/MC.Area above supply and below price.Do not call PS profit.
Tax wedgeGap between buyer price and seller price.Pbuyer – Pseller = t at post-tax Q.Legal payer ≠ economic burden.
Tax incidenceEconomic burden of a tax.Less elastic side bears more burden.Do not reverse elasticity rule.
DWLLost total surplus from blocked beneficial trades.Triangle between Q* and Qt.Tax DWL base is lost trades, not taxed Q.
Zero economic profitNormal return after opportunity costs.Long-run competitive equilibrium condition.Not zero accounting profit.
Theory: Surplus and efficiency

Total surplus = CS + PS. At competitive equilibrium, all units where WTP ≥ MC are traded → efficient. Any policy reducing Q below Q* creates DWL. After a tax: welfare = remaining CS + remaining PS + tax revenue; the triangle lost is DWL.

Long-run competitive equilibrium trap: Entry and exit push economic profit → 0. At zero profit, P = min AC, and since P = MC, we get MC = AC at minimum. So long-run equilibrium condition is P = MC = min AC.

Graphs to redraw (text-only redraw cards)
1. Demand/supply equilibrium
Axes: Q (x), P (y). Draw D downward, S upward. Mark P*, Q*. Label D, S, E. Sentences: Qd=Qs at P*; last unit has WTP=WTA. Trap: do not label shortage at equilibrium.
2. Tax wedge, revenue, DWL
Draw D, S, then S+tax shifted up. Mark Qt, Pbuyer, Pseller. Revenue rectangle = tax × Qt. DWL triangle from Qt to Q*. Trap: use Qt not Q* for revenue.
3. Price ceiling (binding)
Horizontal line below P*. At ceiling price: Qd (right) > Qs (left) = shortage. Trap: ceiling above P* is non-binding.
4. Price floor (binding)
Horizontal line above P*. At floor price: Qs (right) > Qd (left) = surplus. Trap: floor below P* is non-binding.
5. CS and PS triangles
CS: triangle above price, below D, left of Q*. PS: triangle below price, above S, left of Q*. Shade separately. Trap: never swap positions.
6. Price-taking firm P=MC
Horizontal P=MR line at market price. MC curve (U-shape or upward). AC curve. Firm produces where P=MC. Trap: no downward demand for individual price-taker.
Worked examples
A. Equilibrium calculation
Given: Qd = 68 – 0.02P, Qs = 28 + 0.005P. Set equal: 68 – 0.02P = 28 + 0.005P → 40 = 0.025P → P* = 1,600; Q* = 68 – 0.02(1600) = 36. Trap: stop at Q only.
B. Per-unit tax R20
Before: Q=100, P=50. After tax: Qt=80, Pbuyer=62, Pseller=42. Tax revenue = 20 × 80 = R1,600. DWL = ½ × 20 × (100–80) = R200. Trap: use Qt=80 not Q=100.
C. Binding price ceiling at R11
Qd=21, Qs=3 at P=11. Shortage = 21–3 = 18 units. Trap: check if ceiling is below equilibrium first.
D. Price floor surplus
Floor at P=5: Qs=9,000, Qd=5,000. Surplus = 4,000 units. Trap: use floor-price quantities, not equilibrium quantities.
T/F traps for Unit 8
  • A price ceiling binds when it is below equilibrium. (If above → non-binding.) TRUE.
  • Tax revenue uses post-tax quantity. Using pre-tax Q is the classic trap. TRUE.
  • The legal taxpayer always bears the economic burden. FALSE — incidence depends on elasticities.
  • In long-run competitive equilibrium, MC = AC at minimum average cost. TRUE. Entry/exit → zero profit → P = min AC, P=MC, so MC=AC.
  • A demand shift and a movement along demand are the same. FALSE — shift changes all Q at every P; movement follows price change.

Markets — Unit 7: Price-Setting Firms, MR=MC, Monopoly

Unit 7: Monopoly and Price-Setting Firms

Core story

A price-setting firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve. Because lowering price to sell one more unit lowers revenue on all previous units, MR lies below demand. Profit maximisation: set MR=MC, then read price from demand (not MR). Profit = (P – AC) × Q. Markup = P – MC. Monopoly underproduces relative to the efficient output (where D=MC), creating DWL.

Key rules
  • If P = a – bQ, then MR = a – 2bQ (same intercept, double slope).
  • Set MR = MC → solve Q. Then substitute Q into demand → get P.
  • Profit = (P – AC) × Q. Markup = P – MC.
  • Efficient output: where demand = MC (Qeff). DWL = triangle between Qm and Qeff.
  • Elasticity: elastic → MR positive; unit elastic → MR=0; inelastic → MR negative. Monopolist never chooses inelastic portion.
  • TR and elasticity: elastic demand → price cut raises TR. Inelastic demand → price rise raises TR.
Worked example
Full calculation
Given: P = 100 – 2Q, MC = 20, AC = 40 at chosen output.
MR = 100 – 4Q. Set MR=MC: 100–4Q=20 → 4Q=80 → Qm=20.
Read P from demand: P = 100–2(20) = R60.
Profit = (60–40)×20 = R400. Markup = 60–20 = R40.
Efficient Q where D=MC: 100–2Q=20 → Qeff=40. DWL = ½×(60–20)×(40–20) = R400 triangle.
Trap: never read P from MR. Never use P=MC for monopoly.
Graphs to redraw
Price-setting firm
Axes: Q (x), P/cost (y). Draw D downward, MR below D with same intercept and steeper slope. Draw MC and AC. Mark: Qm where MR=MC, read Pm from D. AC at Qm. Profit rectangle from AC to Pm over Qm units. Qeff where D=MC. DWL triangle between Qm and Qeff.
Elasticity along linear demand
Upper half (elastic): TR rises as Q rises / P falls. Midpoint: unit elastic, MR=0. Lower half (inelastic): TR falls as Q falls / P rises. Monopolist produces in elastic region.
T/F traps for Unit 7
  • For a monopolist, MR equals demand. FALSE — MR lies below demand.
  • A profit-maximising monopoly uses P=MC. FALSE — it uses MR=MC then reads P from demand.
  • If demand is elastic, a price increase raises TR. FALSE — elastic: Q falls more than P rises → TR falls.
  • Slope and elasticity are identical. FALSE — elasticity depends on slope and the P/Q ratio.
  • Monopoly underproduces. TRUE — Qm < Qeff where D=MC.
  • AC is minimised where MC crosses AC from below. TRUE.

Markets — Unit 10: Externalities, Public Goods, Market Failure

Unit 10: Externalities and Public Goods

Core story

A negative externality occurs when production/consumption imposes costs on third parties not captured in market prices. Private market ignores MEC → overproduces (Qm > Q*). Fix: Pigouvian tax = MEC at Q*. A positive externality means private demand understates social value → underproduces (Qm < Q*). Fix: Pigouvian subsidy = MEB at Q*.

MSC = MPC + MECTotal marginal social cost includes private and external.MSC ≠ MEC alone.
MSB = MPB + MEBTotal marginal social benefit includes private and external.Do not use for negative externalities.

Pigouvian subsidyEquals MEB at efficient Q*. Closes gap between MSB and MPB.Do not tax a positive externality.
Coase theoremIf property rights are clear and bargaining costs are low, private bargaining can achieve efficient outcome.Fails with many parties or high bargaining costs.
Public goodNon-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce others') and non-excludable (can't prevent access)."Public good" is not "government-provided good".
Club goodNon-rival but excludable (e.g. streaming subscriptions).Not the same as public good.
Common goodRival but non-excludable (e.g. fish in the ocean).Subject to tragedy of the commons.

Full calculation
P=300–Q, MPC=60+Q, MEC=40.
MSC = MPC+MEC = 100+Q.
Private equilibrium: D=MPC → 300–Q=60+Q → 240=2Q → Qm=120.

Markets — Unit 6: Employment Rent and Labour Discipline

Unit 6: Employment Rent

Core story

Employment rent is the economic value of keeping a job compared with losing it. Because effort is costly and not perfectly observable/contractible (incomplete contract), workers have an incentive to shirk. The firm pays a wage above the reservation wage to make job loss costly — creating rent and discipline. A higher wage on the best-response curve is not kindness; it is profit-maximising because it lowers the cost per efficiency unit.

Key definitions
TermMeaningTrap
Employment rentNet job value – reservation option (per period, per unit)Not just the wage.
Reservation optionBest alternative to current job (unemployment benefit, other job)Higher reservation option → less rent.
Incomplete contractEffort level cannot be fully specified and enforced.Do not assume effort is perfectly observable.
Cost per efficiency unitw / eUse e/w when asked for efficiency units per rand.
No-shirk wageWage high enough so job loss is costly enough to deter shirking.Do not say high wages are kindness.
Involuntary unemploymentWorkers would accept jobs at current wage but no jobs available.Not the same as voluntary job refusal.
Worked example
Standard rent calculation
Worker earns R90/hr. Effort and travel costs: R25/hr. Unemployment benefits: R30/hr. Expected unemployment: 10 weeks. Hours per week: 40.

Net job value = 90 – 25 = R65/hr.
Hourly rent = 65 – 30 = R35/hr.
Total rent = 35 × 40 × 10 = R14,000.

This rent is costly to lose → incentive to supply effort even without perfect monitoring.
Traps: using R90 as rent; ignoring costs; mixing hourly/weekly units.
T/F traps for Unit 6
  • Employment rent is simply the wage. FALSE — it is net job value minus reservation option.
  • Higher unemployment benefits always increase employment rent. FALSE — they improve reservation option → reduce rent.
  • A longer expected unemployment duration increases the cost of job loss. TRUE.
  • Labour discipline model assumes effort is perfectly contractible. FALSE.
  • Paying reservation wage gives no employment rent. TRUE.
  • Large firms face principal-agent problems. TRUE — ownership and control can be separated.

Foundations Units 3, 4, 5, 2, 1

Unit 3: Feasible Set, MRS/MRT, Working Hours

Core story

Unit 3 asks: how does a worker choose between free time and consumption under scarcity? The budget constraint shows feasible combinations; indifference curves show preferences. Optimum: MRS = MRT at an interior tangency. When the wage rises: (1) free time becomes more expensive → substitution effect → more work; (2) worker is richer → income effect → may want more free time if it is a normal good. Net effect on hours is ambiguous.

Key definitions
TermPlainExam useTrap
Feasible setAll combinations achievable under the constraint.Contains affordable/attainable choices.Feasible ≠ optimal.
MRSSlope of indifference curve — subjective willingness to trade.Preference trade-off at a point.MRS is NOT the wage or MRT.
MRTSlope of feasible frontier — objective feasible trade-off.MRT = wage in the working-hours model.MRT is NOT a preference.
Substitution effectChange in consumption/free time due to changed relative price, holding utility constant.Higher wage → free time more expensive → less free time.Do not merge with income effect.
Income effectChange due to changed purchasing power.Higher wage → richer → may want more free time if normal.Do not assume it always increases work.
Normal goodDemand rises with income.Free time is usually normal — income effect may reduce hours.Do not ignore normal-good logic.
Graphs to redraw
1. Feasible set / budget constraint
x-axis: free time. y-axis: consumption. Draw downward-sloping line from max-consumption intercept (0 free time) to max-free-time intercept (0 consumption). Shade feasible region below line. Mark one infeasible point above. Trap: do not put hours worked on free-time axis.
2. MRS = MRT optimum
Draw budget constraint. Draw several ICs (convex). Mark tangency point (MRS=MRT). Mark one IC through a non-optimal feasible point to show it is on a lower IC. Every feasible point is not optimal.
3. Wage increase and effects
Draw old budget line (flatter). Rotate to new steeper budget line (same free-time intercept if no unearned income). Mark old optimum (A) and new optimum (B). If B has less free time: substitution dominates. If B has more free time: income effect dominates. Do not assume one outcome.

T/F traps for Unit 3
  • A change in income changes the shape of indifference curves. FALSE — preferences shape ICs; income changes the budget constraint.
  • If a wage rises, every worker must work more hours. FALSE — income and substitution effects can oppose.
  • MRS measures what is feasible in production. FALSE — MRS measures preferences; MRT measures feasibility.
  • At interior optimum, MRS = MRT. TRUE.
  • The opportunity cost of one hour of free time is the wage forgone. TRUE.

Unit 4: Game Theory — Nash, Pareto, Social Dilemmas

Core story and method

Method for finding Nash: For each cell, ask "would Player 1 deviate?" and "would Player 2 deviate?" A Nash equilibrium is where neither player wants to deviate unilaterally. Mark each player's best response (underline or circle within their payoffs for each opponent action). Nash = cell where both players have best response marked.

Social dilemma: Individually rational behaviour leads to a collectively worse outcome. The Nash equilibrium is Pareto inefficient — another feasible outcome makes everyone better off.

Key definitions
TermMeaningTrap
Best responseAction giving highest payoff for one player GIVEN the other player's action.Compare the SAME player's payoffs — not across players.
Nash equilibriumMutual best response — no unilateral profitable deviation.Nash ≠ fair, efficient or highest total payoff.
Dominant strategyBest action regardless of what the other player does.Stronger than Nash — check every opponent action separately.
Pareto improvementAt least one better off, no one worse off.Must check BOTH players.
Pareto efficientNo Pareto improvement is feasible.Efficient can still be very unfair.
Social dilemmaNash leads to outcome worse for all than a cooperative outcome.Nash can be Pareto inefficient.
Worked example: Pollution game
Payoff matrix: Clean vs Pollute
Clean/Clean: (4,4). Clean/Pollute: (1,6). Pollute/Clean: (6,1). Pollute/Pollute: (2,2).

Find Nash:
If Firm B plays Clean → Firm A: Pollute(6) > Clean(4) → A prefers Pollute.
If Firm B plays Pollute → Firm A: Pollute(2) > Clean(1) → A prefers Pollute.
Pollute is Firm A's dominant strategy. By symmetry, Pollute dominates for Firm B too.
Nash = Pollute/Pollute (2,2).

Social optimum: Clean/Clean (4,4) — total payoff 8 > 4 at Nash.
Clean/Clean is a Pareto improvement over Nash (both move from 2→4).
Social dilemma: private incentives lead to (2,2) though (4,4) is better for both.
Trap: do not choose the cell with highest total payoff as Nash.
T/F traps for Unit 4
  • A Nash equilibrium is always a dominant strategy equilibrium. FALSE — Nash needs mutual BR; dominance is stronger.
  • A Nash equilibrium is always Pareto efficient. FALSE — prisoner's dilemma Nash is Pareto inefficient.
  • A dominant strategy is the strategy that gives the largest total payoff. FALSE — it is best for that player against every opponent action.
  • In a payoff matrix, compare one player's payoff with the other player's payoff to find best responses. FALSE — compare same player's payoffs.
  • A social dilemma has no Nash equilibrium. FALSE — it has one, but it is collectively worse.

Unit 5: Angela-Bruno, Institutions, Bargaining and Fairness

Core story

Institutions are the rules determining who can do what and who gets what. The Angela-Bruno model shows how bargaining power affects allocation. The feasible frontier shows technically possible allocations. The biological survival constraint (BSC) is the minimum Angela needs to survive. The reservation indifference curve (RIC) shows Angela's outside option — she must be at least as well off as without the agreement. A take-it-or-leave-it offer from Bruno can be voluntary (Angela accepts) and on the frontier (Pareto efficient) but still unfair if Bruno captures most surplus.

Three tests to separate: Efficiency (on frontier / no Pareto improvement?), Voluntariness (on or above RIC?), Fairness (how is surplus distributed?).

Unit 2: Technology, Opportunity Cost and Isocost

Core story

Unit 2 asks how firms choose technology and what determines production efficiency. Every choice has an opportunity cost — the next best option forgone. Economic rent is the surplus over the next best. Firms choose among technologies based on isocost lines (input-price trade-offs). A dominated technology uses more of every input for the same output and is never chosen. Production exhibits diminishing marginal product in the Malthusian model: more labour eventually raises output less than proportionately.

T/F traps for Unit 2

  • Marginal product is total output divided by total labour. FALSE — that is AP; MP is ΔOutput/ΔInput.

  • The slope of an isocost line depends on relative input prices. TRUE.
  • Economic rent is the same as wage. FALSE — rent is chosen payoff minus next best.

Unit 1: GDP, Capitalism, Inequality and Planetary Limits

Core story

Unit 1 introduces the long-run picture: how capitalism generated sustained growth (the hockey-stick GDP graph), why growth is unequal, and what GDP measures and omits. GDP = market value of final goods and services produced in a country in a period. GDP per capita = GDP ÷ population (an average that hides distribution). PPP adjusts for purchasing power differences across countries. Capitalism requires private property, markets and firms — not zero government. The Malthusian trap: population pressure offsets productivity gains. Planetary limits: growth cannot continue indefinitely on a finite planet.

GDP critique — what to include in answers
  • What GDP measures: paid market production at market prices; a useful summary of economic activity.
  • What GDP omits: unpaid household work, leisure, inequality, environmental degradation, wellbeing dimensions not sold in markets.
  • How to critique without dismissing: "GDP is a useful but incomplete measure. It captures market production but omits [X]. A better measure of wellbeing would also include [Y]."
  • PPP: adjusts money values for local price differences → better cross-country comparison.
  • Hockey-stick graph: GDP per capita was nearly flat for centuries; sharp rise after Industrial Revolution.
v1.3 Premium Section C exemplar: Unit 1 GDP novel application

Question (8–10 marks)

"An IMF research paper finds that redistributing unpaid household work between men and women could raise measured GDP by 4%. Explain, using GDP measurement concepts, why this is possible. In your answer, explain why it would be wrong to say that GDP rises simply because the IMF transfers money, or because previously unpaid household work is automatically counted in GDP."

What the examiner is testing: GDP definition, what is counted in GDP, market production vs non-market production, how unpaid work can enter GDP indirectly, and two common misconceptions.
30-second planning box:
  • Define GDP: market value of paid final output.
  • Unpaid household work is not in GDP (not sold in markets).
  • Redistribution → more market production → higher GDP.
  • Misconception 1: IMF transfer ≠ domestic GDP rise.
  • Misconception 2: Unpaid work not automatically counted.
Full model answer

GDP measures the market value of final goods and services produced in a country during a given period. This means it counts only production that is exchanged in markets at a price. Unpaid household work — such as childcare, cooking and cleaning performed without payment — is generally excluded from measured GDP even if it contributes to household wellbeing, because it does not appear as a market transaction.

The IMF finding is possible because redistributing unpaid work could free up the time of one group to enter paid market employment. If women, for example, currently perform most unpaid household tasks and redistribution shifts some of those tasks to men (or to paid services), women can enter or expand their participation in paid work. This additional paid market production would be counted in GDP. In this way, unpaid work enters GDP indirectly — not because it is now counted directly, but because its redistribution enables more measured market activity.

It would be wrong to say GDP rises simply because the IMF transfers money. An international transfer from the IMF to individuals does not automatically generate new domestic production. GDP measures output, not income transfers. Receiving a transfer payment does not by itself increase the value of goods and services produced in the domestic economy.

It would also be wrong to say unpaid household work is automatically counted in GDP. By definition, GDP excludes non-market production. Even if unpaid household work has high social value, it does not enter GDP unless it is converted into a paid market transaction.

This question illustrates why GDP is an imperfect measure of wellbeing: it may undercount total economic activity when a large share of productive work is unpaid, and it may miss distributional dimensions that affect welfare.

Mark allocation: 2 GDP definition + what counted; 2 mechanism (redistribution → market entry → higher measured output); 2 misconception 1 (transfer ≠ GDP); 2 misconception 2 (unpaid not automatic); 1 GDP limitation conclusion; 1 clarity = 10.

80% vs 100%: 80% defines GDP and says redistribution enables market participation. 100% adds both misconceptions with clear reasoning and GDP-limitation conclusion.

Common lost marks: Saying unpaid work is now counted in GDP; conflating transfer payments with production; no conclusion about GDP as a measure.

Panic version: GDP = market value of paid output. Unpaid work excluded. Redistribution → some tasks shift → frees time for market work → higher GDP. Transfer ≠ GDP rise. Unpaid work not automatically counted.

T/F traps for Unit 1
  • GDP includes unpaid household work. FALSE — GDP measures market production only.
  • GDP per capita can hide inequality. TRUE — it is an average.
  • A country can be capitalist even if government has an economic role. TRUE.
  • PPP helps compare living standards by adjusting for price differences. TRUE.
  • In the Malthusian model, higher living standards always persist. FALSE — population pressure can erode gains.

Unit 8: Per-unit Tax / Price Control Welfare Analysis 10 marks

Question: A city introduces a R20 per-unit tax on bottled water. Before tax: Q=100, P=R50. After tax: Qt=80, Pbuyer=R62, Pseller=R42. Analyse the welfare effect and contrast with a binding price ceiling.

Examiner tests: tax wedge, post-tax quantity, revenue, DWL, incidence, ceiling comparison.

Planning box: Model = Unit 8 competitive market + per-unit tax. Formulas: revenue = tax × Qt; DWL = ½ × tax × lost trades. Graph: D, S, S+tax, Pbuyer, Pseller, revenue rectangle, DWL triangle. Avoid: using Q=100 for revenue.
Full model answer

A per-unit tax creates a wedge between the price paid by buyers and the price received by sellers. Here the wedge is R62 – R42 = R20, matching the tax. The post-tax quantity is 80, so tax revenue is R20 × 80 = R1,600. The tax reduces quantity from 100 to 80, so 20 mutually beneficial trades no longer take place. The deadweight loss is ½ × R20 × 20 = R200. On the graph, the tax shifts the effective supply curve upward by R20 or creates a vertical wedge at Q=80. The revenue rectangle is the R20 wedge over 80 units; the DWL triangle lies between Q=80 and Q=100 where willingness to pay still exceeds seller cost but the tax prevents trade. Economic incidence depends on relative elasticities, not on who legally pays. A binding price ceiling is set below equilibrium; it creates a shortage (Qd > Qs) and quantity traded is limited by the short side. Both policies can reduce surplus, but the tax raises government revenue while the ceiling mainly creates rationing costs.

Marks: 1 model; 2 revenue/DWL; 2 graph; 2 incidence; 2 ceiling contrast; 1 trap avoidance.

Panic: Wedge = R20. Revenue = 20×80 = R1,600. DWL = ½×20×20 = R200. Draw D, S, S+tax, Pbuyer, Pseller. Ceiling → shortage not revenue.

Unit 7: Monopoly MR=MC, Profit and DWL 10 marks

Question: P=100–2Q, MR=100–4Q, MC=20, AC=40 at chosen output. Find Qm, Pm, profit, explain welfare loss.

Full model answer

A price-setting firm faces downward-sloping demand, so MR lies below demand. Set MR=MC: 100–4Q=20 → Q=20 (Qm). Price from demand: P=100–2(20)=R60. Profit = (P–AC)×Q = (60–40)×20 = R400. Markup = P–MC = 60–20 = R40. Efficient output: D=MC → 100–2Q=20 → Qeff=40. DWL = triangle between Qm=20 and Qeff=40 because 20 units with WTP above MC are not produced. Market power means P>MC and underproduction.

Panic: MR=MC → Qm=20. P from demand → R60. Profit = (60–40)×20 = R400. Markup = R40. DWL from Qm to Qeff.

Unit 10: Negative Externality and Pigouvian Tax 10 marks

Question: P=300–Q, MPC=60+Q, MEC=40. Find Qm, Q*, Pigouvian tax, explain overproduction.

Full model answer

MSC = MPC+MEC = 100+Q. Private: D=MPC → 300–Q=60+Q → Qm=120. Efficient: D=MSC → 300–Q=100+Q → Q*=100. Market overproduces by 20 units because MEC is ignored. Pigouvian tax = MEC at Q*=100 → tax = R40. This aligns private cost with social cost. DWL = triangle between Q*=100 and Qm=120.

Panic: MSC=100+Q. Qm=120. Q*=100. Tax=R40 at Q*. Draw D, MPC, MSC, Qm, Q*, DWL.

Unit 6: Employment Rent and Labour Discipline 8 marks

Question: Worker earns R90/hr, effort+travel costs R25/hr, unemployment benefits R30/hr, 10 weeks expected unemployment, 40 hrs/week. Calculate rent, explain labour discipline.

Full model answer

Net job value = 90–25 = R65/hr. Reservation option = R30/hr. Hourly rent = 65–30 = R35. Total rent = 35×40×10 = R14,000. Because effort is not perfectly contractible, the threat of job loss (losing R14,000 of rent) creates incentive to work. The firm pays above reservation wage not from kindness but because it lowers the cost per efficiency unit (w/e) along the best-response curve.

Panic: Net = 90–25=65. Rent/hr=65–30=35. Total=35×40×10=R14,000. Job loss is costly → effort incentive.

Unit 3: Wage Change, MRS/MRT, Substitution and Income Effects 10 marks

Question: Explain how a wage increase affects the budget constraint and why hours worked may rise or fall.

Full model answer

The budget constraint shows feasible combinations of free time and consumption; its slope (MRT) equals the wage. A wage rise rotates the budget line: free time becomes more expensive. MRS is the subjective trade-off (IC slope); at interior optimum MRS=MRT. Substitution effect: higher opportunity cost of free time → less free time → more work. Income effect: richer → may want more free time if it is a normal good → fewer hours. Net effect is ambiguous: hours rise if substitution dominates; fall if income effect dominates.

Panic: Wage=MRT. Wage rise rotates budget line. Substitution → less free time. Income (if normal) → more free time. Net hours: ambiguous.

Unit 4: Nash vs Pareto/Social Optimum 8 marks

Question: Payoffs: Clean/Clean (4,4), Pollute/Clean (6,1), Clean/Pollute (1,6), Pollute/Pollute (2,2). Find Nash, compare with social optimum.

Full model answer

Best responses: if B plays Clean, A prefers Pollute (6>4). If B plays Pollute, A prefers Pollute (2>1). Pollute dominates for A; by symmetry for B. Nash = Pollute/Pollute (2,2). Social optimum = Clean/Clean (total payoff 8 vs 4). Clean/Clean is a Pareto improvement over Nash (both move from 2→4). This is a social dilemma: individual rationality leads to a collectively worse outcome.

Panic: Mark best responses. Pollute dominates → Nash = Pollute/Pollute (2,2). Clean/Clean = social optimum (total 8). Pareto improvement over Nash.

Unit 5: Angela-Bruno Efficiency, Voluntariness and Fairness 10 marks

Question: An allocation is on the feasible frontier and on Angela's reservation IC but gives Bruno most surplus. Is it efficient, voluntary and fair?

Full model answer

Institutions determine who can do what and who gets what. An allocation on the feasible frontier can be Pareto efficient because moving along the frontier to help one person usually hurts the other. If it is on Angela's reservation IC, she is as well off as her outside option — it may be voluntary in the narrow sense. But voluntary does not mean fair. If Bruno makes a take-it-or-leave-it offer, he captures most surplus. The outcome is efficient (on frontier), possibly voluntary (Angela accepts), but substantively unfair (surplus concentrated in Bruno). Procedural fairness asks about the process; substantive fairness asks about the distribution. These three tests must be applied separately.

Panic: On frontier = possibly Pareto efficient. On RIC = possibly voluntary. Unequal surplus = unfair. Efficiency, voluntariness and fairness are separate tests.

Unit 2: Opportunity Cost, Rent and Isocost 8 marks

Question: Explain opportunity cost and economic rent in a production context. Show how input prices determine technology choice.

Full model answer

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative forgone. Economic rent is the payoff from a chosen option minus the next best option. When input prices are w (wage) and r (capital cost), the isocost line shows all input combinations with the same total cost. Its slope is –w/r. A rational firm chooses the technology on the lowest attainable isocost line. A dominated technology uses more of every input for the same output and is never chosen. Changes in relative input prices change the slope of isocost lines and can change the cost-minimising technology.

Panic: OC = next best forgone. Rent = chosen – next best. Isocost slope = –w/r. Choose lowest-cost technology. Dominated techs never chosen.
