Markets — Unit 8: Competitive Markets, Taxes and Price Controls

Unit 8: Competitive Markets

Core story

Unit 8 models markets with many buyers and sellers where no single participant sets price. Demand represents willingness to pay (slopes down). Supply represents willingness to accept/MC (slopes up). Competitive equilibrium: Qd=Qs — market clears. The individual price-taking firm faces horizontal demand at market price and chooses q where P=MC.

Policy: a price ceiling (max price) binds only below equilibrium → creates shortage (Qd>Qs). A price floor (min price) binds only above equilibrium → creates surplus (Qs>Qd). A per-unit tax creates a wedge between buyer price and seller price; post-tax quantity falls; tax revenue = tax × Qt; DWL = ½ × tax × (Q* – Qt).

Theory: Surplus and efficiency

Total surplus = CS + PS. At competitive equilibrium, all units where WTP ≥ MC are traded → efficient. Any policy reducing Q below Q* creates DWL. After a tax: welfare = remaining CS + remaining PS + tax revenue; the triangle lost is DWL.

Long-run competitive equilibrium trap: Entry and exit push economic profit → 0. At zero profit, P = min AC, and since P = MC, we get MC = AC at minimum. So long-run equilibrium condition is P = MC = min AC.

Markets — Unit 7: Price-Setting Firms, Elasticity & Efficiency

Unit 7: Price-Setting Firms, MR=MC, Elasticity & Gains from Trade

Core story — read this first if you haven't seen Unit 7 before

Unit 8 introduced the price-taking firm: so many competitors that no one can affect the market price — you just accept P and choose Q. Unit 7 is the opposite: a price-setting (monopoly or differentiated-product) firm faces the whole market demand curve. It can charge whatever price it likes — but higher price means fewer sales. The demand curve is its constraint.

Because of this, there is a hidden cost to selling one more unit: you have to lower your price for every unit already sold. That makes marginal revenue (MR) always less than price. The gap between demand (= price) and MR is the key to understanding monopoly behaviour.

The 4-step rule for any price-setting firm: (1) Find MR from demand. (2) Set MR = MC → solve for Qm. (3) Substitute Qm into the demand curve → get Pm. (4) Profit = (Pm – AC) × Qm. The efficient output is where demand = MC (not MR = MC) — the monopolist underproduces, creating DWL.

Part A: Why does a price-setting firm exist? Market power explained

A firm has market power when it sells a product with no perfect substitute — a differentiated product. It is the only seller of that specific variety. Examples: a specific smartphone brand, a car model, a local service.

Because buyers cannot instantly switch to an identical alternative, the firm can raise price without losing ALL its customers. This means its demand curve slopes downward (unlike the flat demand for a price-taker). The steeper the demand curve relative to its height, the less market power. The flatter, the more competition constrains the firm.

Price-taker vs price-setter — the critical difference
Price-taker (Unit 8): Faces horizontal demand at market price P. Sets P = MC to choose Q. No market power.
Price-setter (Unit 7): Faces downward-sloping demand. Chooses both Q and P. Sets MR = MC then reads P from demand. Has market power.
Trap: a price-setter does NOT choose P directly — it chooses Q, and demand then tells it what price the market will bear.
Part B: Where MR comes from — the hidden cost of selling more

Suppose a firm's inverse demand is P = 8 000 – 80Q (from the "Beautiful Cars" example in your slides). At Q = 20, price = 6 400 and TR = 128 000. At Q = 21, price = 6 320 and TR = 132 720. MR = 132 720 – 128 000 = 4 720. But price at Q=21 is 6 320. MR (4 720) is far below price (6 320).

Why? Selling the 21st car earns 6 320 — but to sell it you had to drop the price from 6 400 to 6 320, losing 80 on each of the first 20 cars (= –1 600). Net = 6 320 – 1 600 = 4 720. That "lost revenue on previous units" is the MR gap.

The MR formula for any linear demand
If demand is P = a – bQ, then MR = a – 2bQ
Same P-intercept as demand. Slope of MR is twice as steep as demand.
MR intersects the Q-axis at half the Q-axis intercept of demand.
Derivation: TR = P×Q = (a – bQ)×Q = aQ – bQ². Take derivative (or difference): dTR/dQ = a – 2bQ = MR.

Quick check: For P = 100 – 2Q → MR = 100 – 4Q. For P = 60 – 4Q → MR = 60 – 8Q. Pattern: same intercept, double the slope coefficient.

Part C: CS, PS and gains from trade in a price-setting market

These concepts apply everywhere — not just competitive markets. Recall:

  • WTP (willingness to pay): the maximum a buyer would pay. The demand curve shows WTP for each unit.
  • WTA (willingness to accept): the minimum a seller would accept. The MC curve shows WTA for each unit (the cost of producing it).
  • CS = WTP – P for each unit. Summed: area between demand curve and price line.
  • PS = P – WTA for each unit. Summed: area between price line and MC curve.
  • Total surplus (TS) = CS + PS. This is the total gains from trade.
Beautiful Cars worked example (from your lecture slides)
Demand: P = 8 000 – 80Q. MC = 1 344 + 48Q. MR = 8 000 – 160Q.

Profit-max: MR = MC → 8 000 – 160Q = 1 344 + 48Q → 6 656 = 208Q → Qm = 32.
Price: P = 8 000 – 80(32) = $5 440.

CS: Triangle above $5 440 below demand, from Q=0 to Q=32.
CS = ½ × 32 × (8 000 – 5 440) = ½ × 32 × 2 560 = $40 960.

PS: Trapezoid between price ($5 440) and MC curve, from Q=0 to Q=32.
MC at Q=0: 1 344. MC at Q=32: 1 344 + 48(32) = 2 880.
PS = ½ × [(5 440 – 2 880) + (5 440 – 1 344)] × 32 = ½ × [2 560 + 4 096] × 32 = ½ × 6 656 × 32 = $106 496.

TS = $40 960 + $106 496 = $147 456.
Notice the firm captures a much larger share of TS than consumers do. This raises fairness questions — but it is the firm's legal profit-maximising choice.
Part D: Is the monopoly output Pareto efficient? DWL explained

Pareto efficiency means: no way to make anyone better off without making someone worse off. The competitive equilibrium IS Pareto efficient — all mutually beneficial trades happen. But is the monopoly equilibrium at Qm Pareto efficient?

No. At Qm = 32, a 33rd consumer is willing to pay more than it costs to produce a 33rd car (D > MC at Q = 33). Both the firm and that consumer could gain from trading — but the firm refuses because the price cut needed to sell the 33rd car would reduce profit on all 32 existing sales. The firm sacrifices TS to protect its profit margin.

DWL from monopoly — Beautiful Cars
Efficient output (where D = MC): 8 000 – 80Q = 1 344 + 48Q → 6 656 = 128Q → Qeff = 52.
At Qeff = 52: price = MC = 8 000 – 80(52) = 8 000 – 4 160 = $3 840 (competitive price).

DWL = ½ × (Pm – MC at Qm) × (Qeff – Qm)
= ½ × (5 440 – 2 880) × (52 – 32) = ½ × 2 560 × 20 = $25 600

This triangle represents the value of 20 cars that should have been produced and sold (WTP > MC for each) but were not — because the firm restricted output to keep price high.
Trap: DWL is the triangle from Qm to Qeff — NOT the whole profit rectangle.

Why doesn't the firm just lower price to $3 840? It would maximise total surplus — but not its own profit. If it charged $3 840 to everyone, profit = (3 840 – AC) × 52, which is less than (5 440 – AC) × 32. The firm trades off efficiency (TS) for private gain (profit).

Price discrimination: If the firm could charge different prices to different customers (price discrimination), it could sell more units without dropping price on all existing sales. This raises TS — but may be illegal or impractical.

Market failure conclusion: A price-setting firm with market power creates DWL. This is a form of market failure — the market does not produce the efficient quantity. Government may regulate (price caps, competition law) to push output toward Qeff.

Part E: Pareto efficiency vs fairness — don't confuse them

The competitive market equilibrium is Pareto efficient. But efficient ≠ fair. Pareto efficiency only asks: "can we make someone better off without hurting anyone?" It says nothing about whether the distribution of surplus between rich and poor is just.

  • A market can be Pareto efficient and deeply unequal — e.g. if all the surplus goes to one firm.
  • Even competitive equilibrium has limitations: it assumes price-takers, no externalities, and complete contracts (no hidden information). Few real markets satisfy all three.
  • A person with no money cannot participate in market trade — so they get no gains from trade, even if a trade would benefit them and a seller.
Two questions to always separate in Unit 7:
1. Is this allocation efficient? (Does it maximise TS? Are there unexploited gains from trade?)
2. Is this allocation fair? (Is the distribution of surplus between buyers and sellers, or rich and poor, just?)
Competitive equilibrium: efficient ✔ but not necessarily fair. Monopoly: neither fully efficient (DWL) nor necessarily fair.
Part F: Price elasticity of demand — from scratch

Why elasticity matters: Slope of a demand curve tells you how steeply price changes with quantity — but slope depends on units (kilograms vs tonnes, rands vs cents). A unit-free measure is better. That's elasticity.

Price elasticity of demand (PED or ε): How sensitive is quantity demanded to a change in price?

The formula
ε = (−) % change in Qd / % change in P = (−) (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P)

The (−) sign: demand slopes down, so ΔQ and ΔP move in opposite directions — the formula would naturally give a negative number. We report the absolute value as a positive number by convention.

Interpreting ε:
ε > 1: Elastic — Q falls by a larger % than P rises. Buyers are sensitive to price.
ε < 1: Inelastic — Q falls by a smaller % than P rises. Buyers are not very sensitive.
ε = 1: Unit elastic — Q and P change by the same %. TR is maximised at this point.
ε = ∞: Perfectly elastic — any price rise kills all demand (price-taker's demand curve is flat).
ε = 0: Perfectly inelastic — quantity never changes regardless of price (life-saving drug with no substitute).
Worked calculation from lecture slides
Price rises from R12 to R15. Quantity falls from 210 to 190.

%ΔQ = (190 − 210) / 210 × 100 = −20/210 × 100 = −9.52%
%ΔP = (15 − 12) / 12 × 100 = 3/12 × 100 = +25%
ε = (−)(−9.52 / 25) = 0.38

ε = 0.38 < 1 → inelastic. A 1% price increase causes only a 0.38% fall in quantity demanded.
Trap: always use (new − old) / old for % change, not (old − new) / new.
Part G: Elasticity along a linear demand curve

For a straight-line demand curve, the slope is constant — but elasticity changes at every point. This is one of the most-tested concepts in Unit 7.

The pattern to memorise
Think of a linear demand curve from top-left to bottom-right:

Top half (high P, low Q): ε > 1 — elastic. A 1% price cut → more than 1% rise in Q.
At high prices, Q is small, so even a small ΔQ is a large %ΔQ. Elastic = sensitive.

Midpoint (P = half the P-intercept, Q = half the Q-intercept): ε = 1 — unit elastic.
TR = P × Q is MAXIMISED here. MR = 0 at this point.

Bottom half (low P, high Q): ε < 1 — inelastic. A 1% price cut → less than 1% rise in Q.
At low prices, Q is large, so ΔQ is a small % of the large base. Inelastic = not sensitive.

Key exam rule: A monopolist NEVER chooses the inelastic portion. When ε < 1, MR < 0 — cutting price reduces TR. The firm can always raise profit by raising price (reducing Q into the elastic region).
Numerical example (from lecture: P = 8 000 – 80Q)
At Q = 20, P = 6 400: ΔQ = 1, %ΔQ = 1/20 × 100 = 5. ΔP = −80, %ΔP = −80/6 400 × 100 = −1.25. ε = 5/1.25 = 4 → elastic. MR = 8 000 – 160(20) = 4 800 > 0. ✔

At Q = 40, P = 4 800: ε = (1/40)/(80/4 800) = (0.025)/(0.0167) = 1.5 → elastic. MR = 8 000 – 160(40) = 1 600 > 0. ✔

At Q = 70, P = 2 400: ε = (1/70)/(80/2 400) = (0.0143)/(0.033) = 0.43 → inelastic. MR = 8 000 – 160(70) = −3 200 < 0. ✔

Midpoint of demand: Q-intercept = 100, so midpoint Q = 50. At Q = 50: MR = 8 000 – 160(50) = 0. ε = 1. TR is maximised.
Part H: Elasticity, TR and MR — the connection

TR = P × Q. When you lower price to sell more: P falls but Q rises. Whether TR rises or falls depends on which effect wins — and that is exactly what elasticity measures.

Elastic demand → price cuts pay off (TR rises). Firm has little market power — near-perfect substitutes exist.

Why salt taxes work: Salt demand is inelastic (no substitute, essential). A tax raises price but barely reduces quantity. Government collects large revenue and creates little DWL. Exactly why rulers throughout history taxed salt.
Part I: Factors that affect price elasticity

Five factors determine how elastic or inelastic a demand curve is:

  1. Availability of close substitutes. More substitutes → more elastic. (Coke vs "any cola" → Coke demand is more elastic because Pepsi is a close substitute.)
  2. How broadly defined the product. Narrowly defined → more elastic. ("Luxury cars" is more elastic than "vehicles in general" — you can switch to other vehicles.)
  3. Essential vs optional. Essential goods (bread, medicine) → inelastic. Luxuries (holidays, jewellery) → elastic.
  4. Proportion of income spent. A large share of income → more elastic (you search harder for deals). A tiny share → inelastic (you don't bother.)
  5. Time elapsed since price change. More time → more elastic. In the short run, you can't easily adjust. In the long run, you find alternatives (buy a more fuel-efficient car if petrol price stays high).
Part J: Elasticity and market power / markup

There is a precise relationship between elasticity and the monopoly markup (how far above MC the firm prices):

Lerner Index (markup formula)
(P − MC) / P = 1 / ε

Where ε is the price elasticity of demand.

Elastic demand (ε = 4): Markup = 1/4 = 25%. Firm cannot charge far above MC — buyers switch away.
Inelastic demand (ε = 1.25): Markup = 1/1.25 = 80%. Firm can charge far above MC — buyers have no choice.

This explains why pharmaceuticals (inelastic) earn massive markups, while supermarket staples (elastic — many substitutes) have tiny margins.

DWL and elasticity: When demand is inelastic, a price-setting firm can charge a high markup. The DWL triangle is large (big gap between P and MC). When demand is elastic, the firm cannot charge much above MC (buyers leave), so DWL is smaller.

Coase bargaining — property rights, payment range, when it fails
Core logic: If property rights are clearly assigned (either polluter has right to pollute, or victim has right to clean environment) AND transaction costs are low → private bargaining can reach the efficient outcome without government intervention.

Polluter has right to pollute: Victim must pay polluter to reduce output. Victim's max WTP = external damage avoided. Polluter's min WTA = profit foregone by reducing output. Bargaining range exists if WTP > WTA.

Victim has right to clean environment: Polluter must pay victim to allow pollution. Polluter's max WTP = profit from pollution. Victim's min WTA = damage compensation required. Bargaining range: same if WTP > WTA.

Coase theorem: the efficient output is reached regardless of who holds the property right. The distribution of surplus changes, but Q* does not.
8 Coase calculation drills
Coase-1: Factory produces 100 units and causes R40/unit damage to a farmer. Efficient output Q*=80. If factory reduces to 80: profit falls by R300 total. Farmer saves 20×R40=R800 in damage. (a) Is there a bargaining range? (b) Min payment farmer must offer? (c) Max payment farmer would make?
(a) Yes: farmer gains R800 in saved damage; factory loses R300. R800 > R300 → bargaining surplus = R500. (b) Factory's min WTA = R300 (profit foregone). (c) Farmer's max WTP = R800 (damage avoided). Payment range: R300–R800. Bargain is struck somewhere in that range.
Coase-2: Same numbers but factory has right to pollute. (a) Who pays whom? (b) Does efficient quantity change?
(a) Farmer pays factory to reduce output. Payment range R300–R800 (same as above). (b) No — efficient quantity is still Q*=80. Only the direction of payment changes, not the outcome. This is the Coase theorem.
Coase-3: Factory profit from last 20 units = R900 total. Farmer damage from last 20 units = R800 total. Rights assigned to farmer. (a) Does the factory want to bargain? (b) What is the outcome?
(a) Factory's max WTP = R900 (profit from the 20 units). Farmer's min WTA = R800 (damage from 20 units). Factory WTP > farmer WTA → bargaining range R800–R900. Yes, factory pays. (b) Factory produces last 20 units and pays farmer R800–R900. Efficient output maintained.
Coase-4: Factory profit from last 20 units = R500. Farmer damage = R800. Rights assigned to farmer. (a) Does bargaining succeed? (b) What is the efficient outcome?
(a) No — factory's max WTP = R500 < farmer's min WTA = R800. No overlap. Factory cannot profitably compensate farmer. Bargaining fails. (b) Efficient: factory does NOT produce last 20 units (damage R800 > profit R500 → negative net surplus). Government intervention (tax = MEC at Q*) can achieve this.
Coase-5: 500 residents are harmed by factory smoke. Each resident suffers R10 damage. Factory profit from pollution = R2 000. (a) Total damage? (b) Does Coase work? Why/why not?
(a) Total damage = 500 × R10 = R5 000. (b) Coase fails: 500 parties must coordinate to bargain collectively. Transaction costs (organising, negotiating, enforcing agreement among 500) are prohibitively high. Many-parties obstacle → Pigouvian tax is better solution.
Coase-6: Property rights are unclear (no legal framework). Factory and farmer dispute who has the right. (a) Does Coase bargaining work? (b) What is needed first?
(a) No — Coase requires clear property rights as a precondition. Without knowing who has the right, neither party knows whether they should pay or receive compensation. Bargaining cannot begin. (b) Legal establishment of property rights is needed before Coase bargaining can work.
Coase-7: "A Pigouvian tax and Coase bargaining always produce the same quantity Q*. But they differ in who bears the cost. Explain."
Both produce Q* (efficient output). Under Pigouvian tax: producer pays government; government revenue can compensate others or fund public goods. Under Coase: parties bargain to Q*, but payment goes directly between affected parties. Distributional consequences differ. Coase also avoids government measurement errors (government must know MEC correctly for Pigouvian tax).
Coase-8: A negative externality in a market with asymmetric information: the farmer cannot observe exactly how much the factory produces. (a) Does Coase bargaining still work? (b) What problem does this create?
(a) Coase bargaining is complicated. (b) The factory has incentive to claim it produces more than Q* and request higher compensation. The farmer cannot verify production. This is a hidden action (moral hazard) problem — the factory can defect from any agreed reduction without detection. Coase fails without verifiable information.

Unit 6: Employment Rent

Core story

Employment rent is the economic value of keeping a job compared with losing it. Because effort is costly and not perfectly observable/contractible (incomplete contract), workers have an incentive to shirk. The firm pays a wage above the reservation wage to make job loss costly — creating rent and discipline. A higher wage on the best-response curve is not kindness; it is profit-maximising because it lowers the cost per efficiency unit.

Unit 4: Game Theory — Nash, Pareto, Social Dilemmas

Core story and method

Method for finding Nash: For each cell, ask "would Player 1 deviate?" and "would Player 2 deviate?" A Nash equilibrium is where neither player wants to deviate unilaterally. Mark each player's best response (underline or circle within their payoffs for each opponent action). Nash = cell where both players have best response marked.

Social dilemma: Individually rational behaviour leads to a collectively worse outcome. The Nash equilibrium is Pareto inefficient — another feasible outcome makes everyone better off.

Chicken game, coordination game & ultimatum game
Chicken game
Two drivers head toward each other. Swerve = wimpy but safe. Straight = brave but fatal if both go straight.
Payoffs (Swerve,Swerve)=(2,2); (Swerve,Straight)=(1,3); (Straight,Swerve)=(3,1); (Straight,Straight)=(0,0)

Coordination game
Both players want to choose the SAME action. E.g. drive on left/right; choose same meeting place.
Payoffs: (Left,Left)=(3,3); (Right,Right)=(3,3); (Left,Right)=(0,0); (Right,Left)=(0,0)
Nash equilibria: 2 — (Left,Left) and (Right,Right). Both are Pareto efficient. Repeated interaction → conventions emerge.
Different from prisoner's dilemma: cooperation IS equilibrium here.
Ultimatum game
Proposer splits R100. Responder accepts or rejects. If rejected: both get R0.
Standard game theory: proposer offers R1; responder accepts (R1 > R0). In reality: offers < R30 are often rejected (fairness matters).
Implication: people sacrifice material gain to punish unfair offers → preferences include fairness, not just self-interest.
Repeated games and social dilemmas
One-shot prisoner's dilemma: defect is dominant. But if the game repeats indefinitely: "tit-for-tat" (cooperate first, then copy opponent) can sustain cooperation.
Why: shadow of the future — defecting now risks retaliation in all future rounds.
Condition: discount factor not too high (players must care enough about future). Communities, firms, nations sustain cooperation through repeated interaction and reputation.
Exam: "Explain how repeated interaction may solve a social dilemma" → mention shadow of future, credible threat of punishment, low discount rate.
Pareto dominated vs Pareto inefficient — don't confuse
Pareto dominated: Outcome A is Pareto dominated by outcome B if B makes at least one player strictly better off and no player worse off than A. I.e., B is an unambiguous improvement over A.

Pareto inefficient: An outcome is Pareto inefficient if some Pareto improvement exists — some other outcome would make at least one person better off without making anyone worse off.

Pareto efficient: No further Pareto improvement is possible.

Key: the Nash equilibrium in a prisoner's dilemma — (Defect, Defect) = (1,1) — is Pareto inefficient because (Cooperate, Cooperate) = (3,3) is a Pareto improvement. (1,1) is Pareto dominated by (3,3).

Unit 5: Angela-Bruno, Institutions, Bargaining and Fairness

Core story

Institutions are the rules determining who can do what and who gets what. The Angela-Bruno model shows how bargaining power affects allocation. The feasible frontier shows technically possible allocations. The biological survival constraint (BSC) is the minimum Angela needs to survive. The reservation indifference curve (RIC) shows Angela's outside option — she must be at least as well off as without the agreement. A take-it-or-leave-it offer from Bruno can be voluntary (Angela accepts) and on the frontier (Pareto efficient) but still unfair if Bruno captures most surplus.

Three tests to separate: Efficiency (on frontier / no Pareto improvement?), Voluntariness (on or above RIC?), Fairness (how is surplus distributed?).

BSC vs reservation IC — the key distinction
Biological Survival Constraint (BSC): The minimum consumption Angela needs to survive biologically — barely alive. This is a hard physical floor. Angela cannot survive below this level.

Reservation Indifference Curve (RIC): The IC that gives Angela the same utility as her outside option (autonomy — not working for Bruno). This is an economic floor. Angela will not accept a deal that puts her below this IC.

Key distinction: BSC is physical; RIC is economic (utility-based). RIC is typically above BSC because Angela's outside option gives her more than bare survival. In pre-capitalist coercion, Bruno could push Angela to the BSC. With freedom to refuse, Angela's minimum is the RIC.

Exam trap: "BSC and RIC are the same thing." FALSE — BSC = biological minimum; RIC = economic minimum based on outside option.
Technical feasibility vs economic feasibility
Technically feasible: A point on or inside the production frontier. Angela can physically produce that output with that many work hours.

Economically feasible: A point Angela would voluntarily accept — i.e., at or above her reservation IC. Must also be technically feasible.

Economically feasible set: All (leisure, grain) combinations that are (a) technically feasible AND (b) at or above Angela's RIC.

A point can be technically feasible but economically infeasible (Angela would refuse it). E.g., giving Angela zero consumption is technically possible but economically infeasible if she has an outside option > 0.
Pareto-efficient curve and surplus division
Pareto-efficient frontier: The set of all (Angela grain, Bruno grain) allocations where Angela is on her highest IC consistent with economic feasibility. I.e., allocations where Angela's MRS = MRT (she is optimising given the constraint Bruno imposes).

Equivalently: the Pareto-efficient outcomes are those where the total surplus (grain produced minus Angela's minimum) is distributed such that no rearrangement could make both better off.

Total surplus = grain produced − Angela's minimum grain (RIC grain)

Equal surplus split: Angela and Bruno each get half of total surplus above Angela's minimum.
Equal output split: Angela and Bruno each get half of total grain — but this may not equal the equal surplus split.

Exam: distinguish "equal split of surplus" from "equal split of output." If Angela's minimum is non-zero, these differ.
Take-it-or-leave-it offer logic
Scenario: Bruno makes a take-it-or-leave-it offer. He proposes Angela works H hours and receives G grain.

Angela accepts if and only if: G gives her utility ≥ her autonomy point (RIC).

Bruno's optimal offer: He sets G at exactly Angela's RIC grain level (give her the minimum she'll accept), and extracts all remaining surplus (grain produced − G).

If Angela gains bargaining power (e.g., law grants her right to refuse): Bruno must offer at least RIC — no change to Bruno's strategy. But if Angela can make counter-offers, she can push toward equal surplus split.

Key: who makes the take-it-or-leave-it offer determines who captures the surplus. Maximum coercion = Bruno's offer; Maximum Angela power = Angela extracts all Bruno's surplus above his reservation option.
Procedural vs substantive fairness — with examples
Substantive fairness: Judging the OUTCOME. Is the distribution of resources fair? Is it equal? Does each person get what they deserve?
Example: "Angela got 10 grain; Bruno got 20 grain — is that a fair split?" = substantive question.

Procedural fairness: Judging the PROCESS. Was the procedure by which the allocation arose fair, regardless of the outcome?
Example: "Did Angela freely agree to the allocation?" or "Was there equal bargaining power?" = procedural question.

Key insight: A procedurally fair process can produce substantively unfair outcomes. E.g., "voluntary exchange" (procedurally fair) between a very rich and very poor person may still result in a deeply unequal outcome (substantively unfair). This is the core tension in Unit 5.

Why voluntary exchange can still be unfair: Angela may "voluntarily" accept Bruno's offer if her outside option is terrible — she has no real choice. Formally voluntary ≠ genuinely equal.

Unit 1: GDP, Capitalism, Inequality and Planetary Limits

Core story

Unit 1 introduces the long-run picture: how capitalism generated sustained growth (the hockey-stick GDP graph), why growth is unequal, and what GDP measures and omits. GDP = market value of final goods and services produced in a country in a period. GDP per capita = GDP ÷ population (an average that hides distribution). PPP adjusts for purchasing power differences across countries. Capitalism requires private property, markets and firms — not zero government. The Malthusian trap: population pressure offsets productivity gains. Planetary limits: growth cannot continue indefinitely on a finite planet.

GDP critique — what to include in answers
  • What GDP measures: paid market production at market prices; a useful summary of economic activity.
  • What GDP omits: unpaid household work, leisure, inequality, environmental degradation, wellbeing dimensions not sold in markets.
  • How to critique without dismissing: "GDP is a useful but incomplete measure. It captures market production but omits [X]. A better measure of wellbeing would also include [Y]."
  • PPP: adjusts money values for local price differences → better cross-country comparison.
  • Hockey-stick graph: GDP per capita was nearly flat for centuries; sharp rise after Industrial Revolution.

Question (8–10 marks)

"An IMF research paper finds that redistributing unpaid household work between men and women could raise measured GDP by 4%. Explain, using GDP measurement concepts, why this is possible.   • Unpaid household work is not in GDP (not sold in markets).

  • Misconception 1: IMF transfer ≠ domestic GDP rise.
  • Misconception 2: Unpaid work not automatically counted.
Full model answer

GDP measures the market value of final goods and services produced in a country during a given period. This means it counts only production that is exchanged in markets at a price. Unpaid household work — such as childcare, cooking and cleaning performed without payment — is generally excluded from measured GDP even if it contributes to household wellbeing, because it does not appear as a market transaction.

The IMF finding is possible because redistributing unpaid work could free up the time of one group to enter paid market employment. If women, for example, currently perform most unpaid household tasks and redistribution shifts some of those tasks to men (or to paid services), women can enter or expand their participation in paid work. This additional paid market production would be counted in GDP. In this way, unpaid work enters GDP indirectly — not because it is now counted directly, but because its redistribution enables more measured market activity.

It would be wrong to say GDP rises simply because the IMF transfers money. An international transfer from the IMF to individuals does not automatically generate new domestic production. GDP measures output, not income transfers. Receiving a transfer payment does not by itself increase the value of goods and services produced in the domestic economy.

It would also be wrong to say unpaid household work is automatically counted in GDP. By definition, GDP excludes non-market production. Even if unpaid household work has high social value, it does not enter GDP unless it is converted into a paid market transaction.

This question illustrates why GDP is an imperfect measure of wellbeing: it may undercount total economic activity when a large share of productive work is unpaid, and it may miss distributional dimensions that affect welfare.

Unit 5: Angela-Bruno Efficiency, Voluntariness and Fairness 10 marks

Question: An allocation is on the feasible frontier and on Angela's reservation IC but gives Bruno most surplus. Is it efficient, voluntary and fair?

Full model answer

Institutions determine who can do what and who gets what. An allocation on the feasible frontier can be Pareto efficient because moving along the frontier to help one person usually hurts the other. If it is on Angela's reservation IC, she is as well off as her outside option — it may be voluntary in the narrow sense. But voluntary does not mean fair. If Bruno makes a take-it-or-leave-it offer, he captures most surplus. The outcome is efficient (on frontier), possibly voluntary (Angela accepts), but substantively unfair (surplus concentrated in Bruno). Procedural fairness asks about the process; substantive fairness asks about the distribution. These three tests must be applied separately.

80% vs 100%: 80% applies all three tests and states results separately. 100% distinguishes substantive fairness (distribution) from procedural fairness (process), uses BSC vs RIC explicitly, names institutions as the mechanism through which Bruno's bargaining power operates, and links to a policy implication (e.g. labour law).

Panic: On frontier = possibly Pareto efficient. On RIC = possibly voluntary. Unequal surplus = unfair. Efficiency, voluntariness and fairness are separate tests.

Unit 2: Opportunity Cost, Rent and Isocost 8 marks

Question: Explain opportunity cost and economic rent in a production context. Show how input prices determine technology choice.

Full model answer

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative forgone. Economic rent is the payoff from a chosen option minus the next best option. When input prices are w (wage) and r (capital cost), the isocost line shows all input combinations with the same total cost. Its slope is –w/r. A rational firm chooses the technology on the lowest attainable isocost line. A dominated technology uses more of every input for the same output and is never chosen. Changes in relative input prices change the slope of isocost lines and can change the cost-minimising technology.

80% vs 100%: 80% defines OC and rent correctly and states the isocost rule. 100% provides a numeric isocost example (specific w, r, slope calculated), shows a dominated technology with concrete input numbers, and explains how a relative price change shifts the cost-minimising technology choice.

Panic: OC = next best forgone. Rent = chosen – next best. Isocost slope = –w/r. Choose lowest-cost technology. Dominated techs never chosen.

Ruby / Ntsako / Sylvia / Maria / any named worker

Factory (A) causes R300 damage to farmer (B). A earns R500 with pollution, R150 without. What is the Coase bargaining range?

Memo

A pays up to R500−R150 = R350 maximum. B accepts at least R300 (damage). Bargaining range: R300–R350. Agreement is possible → illustrates Coase theorem.

U10-P6: Goods matrix

Classify: (a) National defence, (b) Netflix streaming, (c) Wild fish in ocean, (d) A pizza slice.

Memo

(a) Public good: non-rival + non-excludable. (b) Club good: non-rival + excludable (subscription). (c) Common good: rival + non-excludable. (d) Private good: rival + excludable. Trap: “public” ≠ government-provided.

U10-P7: Moral hazard vs adverse selection (3 scenarios)

(a) Doctors order more tests after switching to salaried pay. (b) Only high-risk drivers buy comprehensive insurance. (c) After buying home insurance, owners install fewer smoke alarms. MH or AS?

Memo

(a) Moral hazard — action changes after contract. (b) Adverse selection — hidden type before contract. (c) Moral hazard — hidden action after contract. Rule: before = AS; after = MH.

U10-P8: Free rider problem (explain)

Why do markets underprovide public goods? Use free riding.

Memo

Public good is non-excludable — once provided, all benefit regardless of payment. Rational agents free ride: wait for others to pay while still receiving the benefit. Private providers cannot recover costs through pricing. Market provision is zero or insufficient. Government provision or regulation needed.

U10-P9: Direction trap (T/F)

True or False: A negative externality causes underproduction; a positive externality causes overproduction.

Memo

False — completely reversed. Negative externality: MPC<MSC → ignores MEC → overproduces (Qm>Q*). Positive externality: MPB<MSB → ignores MEB → underproduces (Qm<Q*). One of the most common exam misconceptions.

U10-P10: Full Section C (10 marks)

Bee-keeper’s bees pollinate neighbours. MPC=5, MPB=80−Q, MEB=20. (a) Qm, Q*. (b) Externality type. (c) Policy + calculation. (d) Graph.

Memo

(a) MSB=100−Q. Qm: 80−Q=5 → Qm=75. Q*: 100−Q=5 → Q*=95. Q*>Qm ✔.
(b) Positive externality.
(c) Subsidy = MEB at Q*=20 → R20/unit.
(d) Graph: MPB down; MSB above MPB (+20 parallel shift); MPC horizontal at 5. Mark Qm=75 (MPB=MPC), Q*=95 (MSB=MPC). Shade DWL between 75 and 95.
Marks: 2 Qm/Q*; 1 type; 2 policy+calc; 2 graph; 2 interpretation; 1 trap.

Unit 3 Practice — 8 items (MRS/MRT, Working Hours, Wage Effects)
U3-P1: MRS vs MRT (T/F)

True or False: MRS is determined by the wage rate; MRT is determined by preferences.

Memo

False — reversed. MRS = slope of IC = subjective preference trade-off. MRT = slope of budget constraint = objective market opportunity cost (= wage in this model). Trap: swapping the two.

U3-P2: Budget constraint equation

Wage w=R200/hr, unearned income V=R400. Write the budget constraint (c as a function of t). Max consumption? Max free time?

Memo

c = 200(24−t)+400 = 5,200−200t. Max c (t=0): R5,200. Max free time: constrained to t≤24; at t=24, c=V=R400. Trap: forgetting unearned income.

U3-P3: Wage increase rotation

Wage rises R150→R250/hr, no unearned income. Describe the budget constraint change.

Memo

Free-time intercept (24h) unchanged. Consumption intercept: 150×24=R3,600 → 250×24=R6,000. Line rotates outward/steeper. MRT rises from 150 to 250. Each free hour now costs more forgone consumption.

U3-P4: Substitution effect direction

Wage rises. Which direction does the substitution effect push free time and hours worked?

Memo

Substitution effect: free time ↓; hours worked ↑. Mechanism: higher wage → higher OC of free time. Holding utility constant (along old IC), worker substitutes away from now-more-expensive free time toward consumption.

U3-P5: Income effect direction

Wage rises. If free time is a normal good, which direction does the income effect push hours worked?

Memo

Income effect: free time ↑; hours worked ↓. Richer worker demands more normal goods including leisure. This is the pure purchasing-power effect. It works against the substitution effect on hours.

U3-P6: Ambiguous net effect

Why is the effect of a wage rise on hours worked ambiguous?

Memo

Substitution pushes toward more work; income effect (normal good) pushes toward less work. Both operate simultaneously. Net hours depend on which dominates. Empirically: low earners tend to increase hours (substitution dominates); high earners may reduce hours (income dominates) → backward-bending labour supply.

U3-P7: Interior optimum (T/F)

True or False: At interior optimum, the slope of the IC equals the slope of the budget constraint.

Memo

True. MRS = MRT at interior tangency. IC slope = MRS; budget line slope = MRT (= wage). Trap: this is the interior condition — corner solutions are different.

U3-P8: Section C (8 marks)

Minimum wage rises from R100 to R160/hr. Will the worker definitely work more hours? Draw the graph.

Memo

Definitely not. Graph: free time (x), consumption (y). Old line slope 100. New line steeper slope 160. Same free-time intercept (24h). Substitution: free time now costs R160/hr → less free time. Income: richer → more free time (if normal). Net is ambiguous.
Marks: 2 graph (axes, rotation, two optima); 1 MRS=MRT; 2 substitution; 2 income; 1 ambiguity.

Unit 6 Practice — 6 items (Employment Rent, Labour Discipline)
U6-P1: Hourly rent

Worker earns R120/hr. Costs R30/hr. Benefit R40/hr. Hourly rent?

Memo

Net = 120−30 = R90. Rent/hr = 90−40 = R50. Trap: using gross wage R120; ignoring costs.

U6-P2: Total rent

From U6-P1 (R50/hr): 35 hrs/week, 8 weeks expected unemployment. Total rent?

Memo

Total rent = 50 × 35 × 8 = R14,000. This is the stake if the worker loses the job — incentive to supply effort.

U6-P3: Cost per efficiency unit

w=R80, e=0.4. Cost/unit? If w rises to R100 and e rises to 0.6, does cost rise or fall?

Memo

80/0.4 = R200/unit. At w=100, e=0.6: 100/0.6 = R166.67 — cost falls. Higher wage raises effort enough to reduce cost per efficiency unit → profit-maximising.

U6-P4: Benefit effect on rent (T/F)

True or False: Higher unemployment benefits always increase employment rent.

Memo

False. Higher benefits improve reservation option → rent = net job value − reservation falls. Workers less deterred by job loss → less discipline incentive. Classic exam trap.

U6-P5: Involuntary unemployment

Explain why involuntary unemployment exists in equilibrium in the labour discipline model.

Memo

Firm pays above reservation wage to create rent and deter shirking (effort not contractible). At profit-maximising wage, Qs > Qd for labour. Workers who would accept current wage cannot find jobs — involuntarily unemployed. Full employment would destroy rent and the effort incentive.

U6-P6: Section C (8 marks)

w=R150, costs=R35, benefit=R50, 12 weeks unemployment, 40 hrs/week. Calculate rent and explain discipline mechanism.

Memo

Net = 115/hr. Rent/hr = 65. Total = 65×40×12 = R31,200. Mechanism: effort unobservable (incomplete contract) → if caught shirking, fired → loses R31,200 → rational to work. Firm sets wage on best-response curve to minimise w/e.
Marks: 1 net; 1 hourly rent; 1 total; 2 mechanism; 1 incomplete contract; 1 best-response; 1 conclusion.

Unit 4 Practice — 6 items (Game Theory, Nash, Social Dilemmas)
U4-P1: Best-response marking

Payoffs (A,B): Up/Left=(5,4); Up/Right=(2,6); Down/Left=(3,7); Down/Right=(6,2). Mark best responses for each player in each opponent scenario.

Memo

A: B=Left → Up(5)>Down(3). B=Right → Down(6)>Up(2). B: A=Up → Right(6)>Left(4). A=Down → Left(7)>Right(2). No cell has both best-responses → no pure Nash equilibrium. Trap: comparing A payoff with B payoff.

U4-P2: Nash equilibrium

Payoffs (A,B): X/X=(3,3); X/Y=(1,4); Y/X=(4,1); Y/Y=(2,2). Find all Nash equilibria.

Memo

A: B=X → Y(4)>X(3). B=Y → Y(2)>X(1). Y dominates A. B: A=X → Y(4)>X(3). A=Y → Y(2)>X(1). Y dominates B. Nash = (Y,Y)=(2,2). Social optimum = (X,X)=(3,3). Prisoner’s dilemma.

U4-P3: Dominant strategy (T/F)

True or False: If both players have a dominant strategy, their intersection is a Nash equilibrium.

Memo

True. Dominant strategy is best regardless of opponent. If both have one, neither wants to deviate from it → by definition a Nash equilibrium.

U4-P4: Multiple Nash equilibria

Payoffs: U/L=(2,2); U/R=(0,0); D/L=(0,0); D/R=(3,3). Find all Nash equilibria.

Memo

(U,L): A→U(2)>D(0); B→L(2)>R(0) → Nash. (D,R): A→D(3)>U(0); B→R(3)>L(0) → Nash. Two Nash equilibria — coordination game, not prisoner’s dilemma.

U4-P5: Pareto improvement identification

Current: (Pollute,Pollute)=(2,2). Alternative: (Clean,Clean)=(4,4). Is the alternative a Pareto improvement? Is the current outcome Pareto efficient?

Memo

(Clean,Clean) IS a Pareto improvement: both move 2→4 (at least one better, none worse). (Pollute,Pollute) is Pareto inefficient — a Pareto improvement exists. Nash can be Pareto inefficient in social dilemmas.

U4-P6: Section C (8 marks)

Payoffs: Not/Not=(4,4); Invest/Not=(1,7); Not/Invest=(7,1); Invest/Invest=(5,5). Find Nash, social optimum, explain social dilemma.

Memo

Not dominates for A (4>1 if B=Not; 7>5 if B=Invest). By symmetry, Not dominates B. Nash = (Not,Not)=(4,4). Social optimum = (Invest,Invest)=(5,5). (5,5) is Pareto improvement over Nash. Social dilemma: each firm rationally defects even though cooperation yields higher payoff for both. Solution: binding agreement, regulation, or tax incentives.
Marks: 2 Nash; 1 social optimum; 2 dilemma; 2 Pareto comparison; 1 solution.

Unit 5 Practice — 6 items (Angela-Bruno, Bargaining, Fairness)
U5-P1: Feasibility tests

Total grain=15. Angela BSC=4, reservation=6. Bruno reservation=3. Assess: (a) A=4, B=11. (b) A=7, B=8. (c) A=10, B=3. (d) A=12, B=2.

Memo

(a) Technical ✔. BSC ✔. Economic: Angela(4)<reservation(6) → not feasible for Angela.
(b) Technical ✔. BSC ✔. Both ≥ reservation → economically feasible.
(c) Technical ✔. BSC ✔. Both ≥ reservation → feasible.
(d) Technical ✔. BSC ✔. Bruno(2)<3 → not feasible for Bruno.

U5-P2: BSC vs RIC (T/F)

True or False: If Angela is at her biological survival constraint she must also be at her reservation indifference curve.

Memo

False. BSC = physical survival minimum. RIC = utility of best outside option. Entirely different concepts. RIC may be above or below BSC. Angela can be alive (above BSC) but economically unwilling to work (below RIC).

U5-P3: Bargaining power effect

Bruno has take-it-or-leave-it power. Economically feasible range: Angela 6–11. What allocation does Bruno choose?

Memo

Bruno offers Angela exactly her reservation value: Angela=6, Bruno=9. He maximises his share subject to Angela accepting. Outcome may be voluntary (Angela accepts) but is substantively unfair (Bruno captures most surplus).

U5-P4: Efficient but unfair

Allocation (A=6, B=9) is on the feasible frontier. Is it Pareto efficient? Is it fair?

Memo

On frontier → likely Pareto efficient (helping Angela requires taking from Bruno). But substantively unfair — Bruno gets 9/15 (60%) while Angela gets only her reservation minimum. Efficiency and fairness are logically separate judgements.

U5-P5: Procedural vs substantive fairness

Distinguish procedural and substantive fairness in the Angela-Bruno context.

Memo

Procedural: was the process fair? Were rules and property rights legitimate? Substantive: is the distribution fair? A transaction can be procedurally fair (Angela freely consented) but substantively unfair (Bruno gets most surplus). Both are separate normative judgements to apply independently.

U5-P6: Numeric surplus split

Total grain=20. Angela reservation=7, Bruno reservation=5. (a) Total surplus? (b) Equal surplus split → how much each? (c) Is 50/50 grain split an equal surplus split?

Memo

(a) Surplus = 20−7−5 = 8 units. (b) Equal surplus: each gets 4 above reservation. Angela=7+4=11; Bruno=5+4=9. (c) 50/50 grain (10 each): Angela surplus=10−7=3; Bruno surplus=10−5=5 → unequal surplus. Equal grain ≠ equal surplus when reservations differ.

Unit 2 Practice — 6 items (Technology, Opportunity Cost, Isocost)
U2-P1: Opportunity cost

Student spends 3 hours studying instead of earning R90/hr. What is the opportunity cost?

Memo

OC = next best alternative forgone = 3 × R90 = R270. Trap: listing all alternatives instead of only the best one; adding multiple forgone options.

U2-P2: Economic rent

Nurse earns R8,000/month. Next best job pays R6,000. Reservation value R5,500. Calculate economic rent.

Memo

Rent = chosen − next best = 8,000 − 6,000 = R2,000/month. Reservation value determines feasibility, not the rent calculation. Trap: using reservation (R5,500) instead of next best (R6,000).

U2-P3: AP vs MP

At L=10: Q=500. At L=15: Q=650. Calculate MP and AP at L=15.

Memo

MP = ΔQ/ΔL = (650−500)/(15−10) = 150/5 = 30. AP at L=15 = 650/15 = 43.3. Note: AP>MP here → MP is pulling AP down. Trap: swapping formulas.

U2-P4: Isocost slope

w=R600, r=R1,800. Write the isocost equation and calculate slope (K vertical, L horizontal).

Memo

Total cost C = 600L + 1800K. Slope = −w/r = −600/1800 = −1/3. Trap: using −r/w = −3 (inverted) or confusing with marginal product ratio.

U2-P5: Technology choice

Tech A: 4L+2K. Tech B: 2L+4K. Tech C: 3L+3K. w=R300, r=R150. Minimum cost technology?

Memo

Cost A = 4(300)+2(150) = R1,500. Cost B = 2(300)+4(150) = R1,200. Cost C = 3(300)+3(150) = R1,350. Tech B minimises cost. If wages rise relative to capital, shift toward capital-intensive technologies.

U2-P6: Dominated technology (T/F)

Tech X: 5L+3K to produce 100 units. Tech Y: 5L+4K to produce 100 units. Is Y dominated?

Memo

Yes. Y uses more K (4>3) and same L for same output. For any positive r, Y costs more than X. Dominated technology: same or more of every input for same or less output → never chosen by a rational firm.

Unit 1 Practice — 6 items (GDP, Capitalism, Inequality, Planetary Limits)
U1-P1: GDP vs wellbeing

Why is GDP per capita a limited measure of wellbeing? Give two specific omissions.

Memo

GDP measures market value of paid final output only. Omissions: (1) Unpaid household/care work — contributes to wellbeing but excluded from market measurement. (2) Inequality — GDP per capita is an average; it rises even if all gains go to top 1%. Other valid: environmental degradation, leisure, subjective wellbeing. Trap: dismissing GDP entirely — it is useful but incomplete.

U1-P2: GDP per capita and growth rate

GDP = R4,500,000,000. Population = 15,000,000. Calculate GDP per capita. GDP rises to R4,725,000,000 next year. Growth rate?

Memo

GDP per capita = 4.5bn/15m = R300. Growth rate = (4.725−4.5)/4.5 × 100 = 5%. Trap: dividing by new value; forgetting ×100.

U1-P3: PPP adjustment

Country A: USD 10,000 per capita. Country B: USD 8,000 per capita at market rates. After PPP: Country B = USD 12,000. What does this mean?

Memo

PPP adjusts for price level differences. At market rates A appears richer. After PPP, a dollar in B buys more real goods than in A → B residents have higher real purchasing power. PPP is better for comparing real living standards. Trap: using market exchange rates as a proxy for purchasing power.

U1-P4: Unpaid work and GDP (T/F)

True or False: If a parent quits paid work to care for their child at home, GDP falls but wellbeing may not fall.

Memo

True. Quitting paid work removes wage from GDP (paid market production falls). But childcare continues — just unpaid. Family wellbeing may be unchanged or improved. Illustrates why GDP measures market activity, not welfare directly.

U1-P5: Capitalism definition (MCQ)

Which is NOT required for capitalism? (a) Private property. (b) Labour markets. (c) Firms. (d) Zero government intervention.

Memo

(d) Zero government intervention is not required. Capitalism is defined by private property, markets, and firms. Government intervenes in all capitalist economies. Trap: equating capitalism with laissez-faire.

U1-P6: Hockey-stick and Malthus

Why was GDP per capita nearly flat for centuries, then rose sharply? What did Malthus predict and why was he wrong long-run?

Memo

Flat era: any productivity gain was eroded by population growth (Malthusian trap). Malthus: higher income → population rises → living standards return to subsistence. He was right about the mechanism but wrong about the pace of technological change. Industrial Revolution innovations outpaced population growth → persistent real living standard rise. The hockey-stick kink marks sustained productivity exceeding the Malthusian trap.

Section C 1 (10 marks)

A firm faces demand P=80–Q and MC=20. Identify the market structure, find profit-maximising Q and P, calculate profit if AC=30 at that output, and explain the welfare loss.

Model answer

Price-setting firm: MR=80–2Q. Set MR=MC: 80–2Q=20 → Q=30. P from demand: P=80–30=R50. Profit=(50–30)×30=R600. Qeff where D=MC: 80–Q=20 → Q=60. DWL = triangle from Qm=30 to Qeff=60 where P>MC. Market power creates underproduction relative to social optimum.

Section C 2 (10 marks)

Explain why a competitive market with a negative externality overproduces and how a Pigouvian tax corrects this. Use MSC, MPC, MEC and label a graph.

Model answer

Private market equilibrium: D=MPC → Qm. Efficient output: D=MSC=MPC+MEC → Q* < Qm. Market overproduces because producers ignore MEC. Pigouvian tax = MEC at Q*. Tax shifts effective supply upward by MEC, reducing Q from Qm to Q*. DWL eliminated at Q*. Graph: D downward, MPC upward, MSC above MPC. Qm at D=MPC. Q* at D=MSC. Tax wedge = MEC. DWL triangle between Q* and Qm.

Section C 3 (8 marks)

A worker's wage rises. Using the labour-leisure model, explain why hours worked might fall. Draw the graph and separate substitution from income effects.

Model answer

Budget constraint: consumption on y-axis, free time on x-axis. Wage = slope (MRT). Wage rise rotates budget line steeper at free-time axis. Old optimum A: MRS=MRT. New budget line: steeper. Substitution effect: free time more expensive → move along old IC toward less free time → more work. Income effect: richer → demand more normal goods including free time → less work. If income effect dominates: new optimum B has more free time than A → hours worked fall.

Section C 4 (8 marks)

Two firms (A and B) face: Clean/Clean (6,6), Pollute/Clean (8,2), Clean/Pollute (2,8), Pollute/Pollute (3,3). Find the Nash equilibrium and explain why cooperation is difficult without binding agreements.

Model answer

Best responses: If B=Clean: A prefers Pollute (8>6). If B=Pollute: A prefers Pollute (3>2). Pollute dominates for A. By symmetry Pollute dominates for B. Nash = Pollute/Pollute (3,3). Social optimum = Clean/Clean (total 12 vs 6). Clean/Clean is Pareto improvement. Without binding agreements, each firm's dominant incentive is to Pollute regardless of what the other does. This is a social dilemma: private rationality leads to a collectively worse outcome than cooperation would achieve.
