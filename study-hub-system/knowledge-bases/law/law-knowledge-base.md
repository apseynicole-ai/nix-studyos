## 1. Case Structure

### 1.1 Case Name Format (Australian)

```
[Plaintiff/Appellant] v [Defendant/Respondent] [Year] Court Reporter Page
```

Examples:
- `Donoghue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562`
- `Mabo v Queensland (No 2) (1992) 175 CLR 1`
- `R v Hicks [2024] HCA 12`

### 1.2 Case Name Rules
- Use italics for case names in text
- When citing in parentheses: `(Donoghue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562)`
- For repealed cases: include the original year, not the reprint year
- For unreported decisions: include court, docket number, and date

### 1.3 Party Name Conventions
- `R` = Regina/Rex (the Crown in criminal matters)
- `v` = versus (always lowercase in body text; formatted as "v" not "vs")
- "In re" or "Re" = In the matter of (probate, insolvency)

## 2. Case Summary Structure

Every case summary in the hub must address these elements in order:

| Element | Required | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Case name | Yes | Full citation |
| Court | Yes | Full court name, not abbreviation |
| Year | Yes | Decision year |
| Area of law | Yes | Specific — e.g. "Contract — Consideration", not just "Contract" |
| Facts | Yes | Only relevant facts; 2–4 sentences |
| Issue | Yes | The precise legal question: "Whether…" |
| Judgment | Yes | Who won; brief reason |
| Ratio decidendi | Yes | The binding legal rule |
| Key principle | Yes | Exam-ready statement in one sentence |
| Obiter dicta | If significant | Clearly labelled as obiter |

### Distinguishing Ratio from Obiter

**Ratio decidendi:** The legal rule without which the case would have been decided differently. It is binding on lower courts.

**Obiter dicta:** Remarks made in passing — hypothetical scenarios, alternative reasoning, comments on facts not before the court. Not binding but may be persuasive.

**Test:** "Was this rule necessary to decide the actual facts before the court?" If yes → ratio. If no → obiter.

## 3. FIRAC Method

### Structure
```
F — Facts:       The legally relevant facts of the problem
I — Issue:       The precise legal question raised by the facts
R — Rule:        The applicable legal principle and its source
A — Application: Apply the rule to the facts, element by element
C — Conclusion:  "Therefore, [party] will/will not succeed because…"
```

### Issue Framing
The issue must be specific. Avoid generic statements.

Poor: "The issue is whether there is a contract."  
Better: "The issue is whether X's conduct constitutes a valid offer capable of acceptance."

### Application Section
- Work through every element of the legal test
- Acknowledge counter-arguments
- Cite the specific case or statutory provision that supports each point
- Do not introduce new rules in the application — that belongs in the Rule section

### Conclusion
- One sentence
- Direct answer to the issue
- Reference the legal reason
- "Therefore, X will succeed in establishing consideration because…"

## 4. Citation Guide (Australian)

### Reported Decisions
```
[Year] Volume Reporter StartPage
(Year) Volume Reporter StartPage
```
- Square brackets `[ ]` when year is essential to finding the report
- Round brackets `( )` when volume number is sufficient

### High Court of Australia
`(Year) CLR Page` — Commonwealth Law Reports  
`[Year] HCA N` — High Court of Australia (medium neutral)

### Federal Court of Australia
`[Year] FCA N` — Federal Court  
`[Year] FCCA N` — Federal Circuit Court

### State Supreme Courts
- NSW: `[Year] NSWSC N` or `(Year) NSWLR Page`
- Victoria: `[Year] VSC N` or `(Year) VR Page`
- Queensland: `[Year] QSC N` or `(Year) Qd R Page`

### AustLII Neutral Citations
`[Year] [Court abbreviation] [Number]`  
Example: `[2024] HCA 12`

## 5. Court Hierarchy (Australia — Commonwealth)

```
High Court of Australia (final appellate jurisdiction)
         ↑
Federal Court of Australia | State Supreme Courts (in their appellate jurisdictions)
         ↑
Federal Circuit and Family Court | State Supreme Courts (first instance)
         ↑
State District / County Courts
         ↑
State Magistrates' Courts / Local Courts
```

### Binding Precedent Rules
- High Court decisions bind all Australian courts
- Full Court of the Federal Court binds single Federal Court judges
- State Court of Appeal binds state courts below it
- A court is not strictly bound by courts of equal jurisdiction (though persuasive)
- Obiter dicta are persuasive, never binding

## 6. Statutory Interpretation

### Approaches
| Approach | Description | When Used |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Literal rule | Read the plain, natural meaning of the words | When text is clear and unambiguous |
| Golden rule | Modify literal meaning to avoid absurdity | When literal reading produces absurd result |
| Mischief rule | What problem was the statute designed to remedy? | When determining legislative purpose |
| Purposive approach | What was Parliament trying to achieve? | Modern preferred approach in Australia |

### Aids to Interpretation
**Intrinsic aids** (within the Act):
- Long title
- Preamble
- Headings
- Definition sections
- Objects clauses

**Extrinsic aids** (outside the Act, used to resolve ambiguity):
- Second reading speeches
- Explanatory memoranda
- Law Reform Commission reports
- Previous case law

### Key Australian Statutory Interpretation Provision
*Acts Interpretation Act 1901* (Cth) s 15AA: Prefer interpretation that promotes the purpose or object of the Act.

## 7. Legal Writing Standards

### Sentence Structure
- Short, declarative sentences in legal analysis
- One proposition per sentence
- Active voice where possible: "The court held" not "It was held"

### Case References in Text
First reference: Full case name and citation  
Subsequent references: Short form (`Donoghue` or `Carlill`)

### Avoiding Common Errors
| Error | Correct Usage |
|-------|---------------|
| "The case of X held…" | "In X, the court held…" |
| "X proves…" | "X establishes…" or "X supports…" |
| "As per the law…" | State the specific rule |
| "It is argued that…" (vague) | "X will argue that…" (specific) |
| "The ratio is that…" (loose) | State the ratio precisely as a legal rule |

## 8. Essay Question Types (Common)

| Type | Structure Required |
|------|--------------------|
| "Discuss" | Identify issues; apply law; reach balanced conclusion |
| "Critically analyse" | Identify, apply, then evaluate the law — advantages/limitations |
| "Advise [party]" | FIRAC for each issue; practical conclusion |
| "Compare and contrast" | Systematic point-by-point comparison |
| "To what extent…" | Thesis; analysis; measured conclusion with qualifications |

---

## 9. Problem Question Types (Common)

| Fact Pattern | Likely Issues |
|-------------|---------------|
| Person receives offer, delays response | Revocation, lapse of time, postal acceptance rule |
| Person relies on a statement that turns out to be false | Misrepresentation, estoppel |
| Agreement on unusual terms | Incorporation, exclusion clauses, reasonableness |
| Written agreement signed without reading | Signature rule, non est factum |

## 10. Common Law vs Statute — Priority

When a statute and common law rule conflict:
- The statute prevails
- But courts interpret statutes narrowly to preserve common law rights where possible
- Clear, unambiguous statutory language overrides the common law
