A3 Format and Negative Marking Strategy

Section A: True/False

Past A3 papers show negative marking: correct answers earn marks, wrong answers lose marks. Answer when you can state the correction rule in one sentence. Leave blank when you are guessing between two concepts.

Section B: MCQ

Eliminate options by model first: price-setting or price-taking, private or social, movement or shift, Nash or Pareto. Do not pick an option because it sounds familiar; pick it because the graph/formula confirms it.

Section C

Show calculations. Label axes. State the formula before substituting. For graph questions, marks come from axes, curves, equilibrium, policy/social outcome, shaded welfare area and interpretation.

Decision rules
  • Answer T/F if you can identify the exact trap.
  • Skip short questions when all you have is a vibe and negative marking applies.
  • For MCQs, eliminate options that use the wrong model.
  • For graphs, write the condition next to the graph: MR=MC, Qd=Qs, MSC=MPC+MEC, MRS=MRT.
  • For calculations, write units and use post-policy quantities after taxes or price controls.

Unit 8: Per-unit Tax / Price Control Welfare Analysis 10 marks

A. Question wording: A city introduces a R20 per-unit tax on bottled water. Before the tax, equilibrium is Q=100 and P=R50. After the tax, quantity falls to Q=80, buyers pay R62 and sellers receive R42. Analyse the welfare effect of the tax and explain how this differs from a binding price ceiling.

B. What the examiner is testing: Competitive equilibrium, tax wedge, post-tax quantity, tax revenue, DWL, incidence and binding price-control comparison.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: Unit 8 competitive market with a per-unit tax.
  • Write the formulas: tax revenue = tax x Q after tax; DWL = 1/2 x tax x lost trades.
  • Draw: D, S, S+tax, Q*, Qt, Pbuyer, Pseller, revenue rectangle and DWL triangle.
  • Interpret: tax reduces trades and total surplus; a price ceiling creates shortage instead of revenue.
  • Avoid: using the original quantity for tax revenue.
D. Full model answer

A per-unit tax creates a wedge between the price paid by buyers and the price received by sellers. Here the wedge is R62 - R42 = R20, matching the tax. The post-tax quantity is 80, so tax revenue is R20 x 80 = R1,600. The tax reduces quantity from 100 to 80, so 20 mutually beneficial trades no longer take place. The deadweight loss is 1/2 x R20 x 20 = R200. On the graph, demand slopes downward and supply slopes upward; the tax shifts the effective supply curve upward by R20 or creates a vertical wedge between buyer and seller prices at Q=80. The revenue rectangle is the R20 wedge multiplied by 80 units, while the DWL triangle lies between Q=80 and Q=100 where willingness to pay still exceeds seller cost but the tax prevents trade. The economic incidence of the tax depends on relative elasticities, not on who legally pays. A binding price ceiling is different: it is set below equilibrium price, creates a shortage because Qd exceeds Qs, and quantity traded is normally limited by the short side of the market. Both policies can reduce surplus, but the tax raises government revenue while the ceiling mainly creates rationing and search costs.

E. Mark allocation: 1 model; 2 revenue/DWL calculation; 2 graph labels; 2 incidence/welfare interpretation; 2 price ceiling comparison; 1 trap avoidance.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer gets the wedge, revenue, DWL and basic graph right. A 100% answer adds incidence logic, lost-trade interpretation and a clean contrast with binding price ceilings.

G. Common lost marks: using Q=100 for revenue; not labelling Pbuyer and Pseller; calling tax revenue DWL; saying legal burden equals economic burden; mixing up ceiling and floor.

H. Panic version: Tax wedge = R20. Revenue = 20 x 80 = R1,600. DWL = 1/2 x 20 x (100-80) = R200. Draw D, S, S+tax, Pbuyer, Pseller, Qt and Q*. A ceiling below equilibrium causes shortage rather than tax revenue.

I. Active recall version

Question: Analyse the R20 tax and contrast it with a binding price ceiling.

Hidden answer: name the tax wedge, calculate revenue and DWL using post-tax quantity, label the graph, explain incidence and contrast with shortage under a binding ceiling.

Unit 7: Monopoly / Price-setting Firm MR=MC, Profit and DWL 10 marks

A. Question wording: A price-setting firm faces P = 100 - 2Q, MR = 100 - 4Q, MC = 20 and AC = 40 at the chosen output. Find Qm, Pm, profit and explain the welfare loss from market power.

B. What the examiner is testing: Downward-sloping demand, MR below demand, MR=MC, price from demand, profit rectangle, markup and monopoly DWL.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: Unit 7 price-setting firm.
  • Write the formulas: MR=MC; profit=(P-AC)Q; markup=P-MC.
  • Draw: D, MR, MC, AC, Qm, Pm, AC at Qm, profit rectangle, Qeff and DWL.
  • Interpret: market power means P>MC and underproduction.
  • Avoid: reading price from MR.
D. Full model answer

A price-setting firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve, so marginal revenue lies below demand because lowering price to sell one more unit also lowers the price on previous units. Profit maximisation requires MR=MC, not P=MC. Setting 100 - 4Q = 20 gives 80 = 4Q, so Qm = 20. The firm then reads price from demand: Pm = 100 - 2(20) = R60. Since AC at Qm is R40, profit is (P - AC) x Q = (60 - 40) x 20 = R400. The markup is P - MC = 60 - 20 = R40, showing market power. On the graph, the firm chooses Qm where MR intersects MC, moves vertically to the demand curve to find Pm, and draws the profit rectangle between Pm and AC over Qm units. The efficient quantity would be where demand equals MC: 100 - 2Q = 20, so Qeff = 40. Because the monopolist produces only 20 units, units between 20 and 40 have willingness to pay above marginal cost but are not sold. This underproduction creates DWL. The conclusion is that the firm may earn profit, but the market outcome is inefficient because P exceeds MC.

E. Mark allocation: 2 MR=MC and Qm; 1 price from demand; 2 profit/markup; 2 graph labels; 2 DWL explanation; 1 conclusion.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer solves Q and P correctly. A 100% answer explains why MR is below demand, separates markup from profit and identifies Qeff/DWL.

G. Common lost marks: using P=MC; reading P from MR; using MC instead of AC in profit; saying monopoly overproduces; forgetting Qeff.

H. Panic version: Set MR=MC: 100 - 4Q = 20, so Qm=20. Price from demand: P=60. Profit=(60-40)x20=R400. Markup=60-20=R40. Monopoly creates DWL because Qm

I. Active recall version

Question: Solve the monopoly and explain welfare loss.

Hidden answer: MR=MC gives Qm; demand gives Pm; profit uses AC; markup uses MC; DWL comes from Qm below D=MC output.

Unit 10: Negative Externality and Pigouvian Tax 10 marks

A. Question wording: A factory produces a good with demand P=300-Q, private marginal cost MPC=60+Q and marginal external cost MEC=40. Find the private and efficient quantities and explain the Pigouvian tax.

B. What the examiner is testing: MPC, MEC, MSC, private equilibrium, social optimum, Pigouvian tax, overproduction and DWL.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: negative production externality.
  • Write the rule: MSC = MPC + MEC; private equilibrium D=MPC; efficient output D=MSC.
  • Draw: D/MB, MPC, MSC, Qm, Q*, tax wedge at Q* and DWL.
  • Interpret: private market overproduces because external cost is ignored.
  • Avoid: setting tax equal to total external damage.
D. Full model answer

A negative externality occurs when production imposes a cost on others that is not fully paid by the producer. The private market uses MPC, while society uses MSC = MPC + MEC. The private equilibrium is where demand equals MPC: 300 - Q = 60 + Q, so 240 = 2Q and Qm = 120. Social marginal cost is MSC = 60 + Q + 40 = 100 + Q. The efficient quantity is where demand equals MSC: 300 - Q = 100 + Q, so 200 = 2Q and Q* = 100. The market overproduces by 20 units. A Pigouvian tax should equal the marginal external cost at the efficient quantity; here MEC is constant at R40, so the efficient tax is R40 per unit. On the graph, MSC lies above MPC by the MEC. Qm is where D intersects MPC, while Q* is where D intersects MSC. The DWL is the triangle between Q* and Qm where MSC exceeds marginal benefit. The policy conclusion is that the tax internalises the external cost, moves private decision-making toward the social optimum and reduces overproduction, although fairness and compensation issues may remain.

E. Mark allocation: 1 definition; 2 private Q; 2 efficient Q; 1 Pigouvian tax; 2 graph labels/DWL; 2 policy interpretation.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer finds Qm, Q* and the tax. A 100% answer explains why MSC is above MPC, names overproduction and discusses policy meaning.

G. Common lost marks: MSC=MEC; tax at Qm; tax equals total damage; missing DWL; saying negative externality underproduces.

H. Panic version: Negative externality: MSC=MPC+MEC. Qm from D=MPC is 120. Q* from D=MSC is 100. Pigouvian tax=MEC at Q*=R40. Draw D, MPC, MSC, Qm, Q*, tax wedge and DWL.

I. Active recall version

Question: Find Qm, Q* and the Pigouvian tax.

Hidden answer: D=MPC gives private output; D=MSC gives efficient output; tax equals MEC at Q*; graph shows overproduction and DWL.

Unit 6: Employment Rent and Labour Discipline 8 marks

A. Question wording: A worker earns R90/hour, has effort and travel costs of R25/hour, receives unemployment benefits of R30/hour and expects 10 weeks of unemployment if dismissed. She works 40 hours per week. Calculate employment rent and explain how it disciplines effort.

B. What the examiner is testing: Employment rent, reservation option, net job value, time units, incomplete contracts and labour discipline.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: Unit 6 employment rent.
  • Write the formula: hourly rent = net job value - reservation option; total rent = hourly rent x hours x weeks.
  • Draw if needed: best response, wage, effort, reservation option and isocost.
  • Interpret: job loss is costly, so workers supply effort.
  • Avoid: treating rent as wage.
D. Full model answer

Employment rent is the economic rent from keeping a job compared with the next best alternative, usually unemployment or another reservation option. The worker earns R90/hour but has effort and travel costs of R25/hour, so net job value is R65/hour. Her reservation option is unemployment benefits of R30/hour. Hourly employment rent is therefore R65 - R30 = R35/hour. Over a 40-hour week for 10 expected weeks of unemployment, total employment rent is R35 x 40 x 10 = R14,000. This rent matters because effort is costly and not perfectly contractible: the firm cannot write a complete contract specifying every level of effort. If the worker is dismissed for low effort, she loses the rent, so the threat of job loss creates an incentive to work. In the labour discipline graph, the firm chooses a wage on the best response curve that induces enough effort at the lowest cost per efficiency unit. The conclusion is that wages above the reservation wage can be profit-maximising because they create discipline, not because the firm is being kind.

E. Mark allocation: 2 net hourly rent; 2 total rent; 2 incomplete-contract mechanism; 1 graph language; 1 conclusion.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer gets the rent calculation and says job loss is costly. A 100% answer connects rent to incomplete contracts, effort incentives and the best-response/isocost graph.

G. Common lost marks: using R90 as rent; ignoring costs; ignoring benefits; mixing hourly/weekly units; saying high wages are kindness.

H. Panic version: Net job value=90-25=R65. Hourly rent=65-30=R35. Total rent=35 x 40 x 10=R14,000. Because job loss is costly, the worker has an incentive to supply effort when effort is hard to monitor.

I. Active recall version

Question: Calculate employment rent and explain labour discipline.

Hidden answer: subtract effort/travel and benefits consistently, multiply by expected unemployment duration, then link rent to costly job loss and effort incentives.

Unit 3: Wage Change, MRS/MRT, Substitution and Income Effects 10 marks

A. Question wording: A worker chooses between free time and consumption. Explain how a wage increase affects the budget constraint and why hours worked may rise or fall.

B. What the examiner is testing: Feasible set, budget constraint slope, opportunity cost of free time, MRS, MRT, substitution effect, income effect and ambiguity.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: labour-leisure choice.
  • Write the rule: optimum where MRS=MRT at an interior tangency.
  • Draw: free time x-axis, consumption y-axis, old/new budget lines, ICs and old/new optimum.
  • Interpret: wage rise has substitution and income effects.
  • Avoid: saying higher wages always increase hours.
D. Full model answer

The worker faces scarcity because more free time means less paid work and therefore less consumption. The budget constraint shows the feasible combinations of free time and consumption, with free time on the horizontal axis and consumption on the vertical axis. The wage is the opportunity cost of free time: one extra hour of free time means one less hour of work and forgone consumption equal to the wage. MRT is the feasible trade-off given by the slope of the constraint, while MRS is the worker's willingness to trade consumption for free time along an indifference curve. At an interior optimum, MRS=MRT, so the worker's subjective trade-off equals the market or technical trade-off. When the wage rises, the budget line becomes steeper because free time is more expensive. The substitution effect makes the worker choose less free time and more work. But if free time is a normal good, the income effect from being richer may make the worker want more free time and fewer hours. Therefore the net effect on hours is ambiguous: hours rise if the substitution effect dominates and fall if the income effect dominates. A top answer separates preferences from constraints and explains both effects rather than assuming one direction.

E. Mark allocation: 2 graph/constraint; 2 MRS vs MRT; 2 optimum condition; 2 substitution/income effects; 1 ambiguity; 1 trap avoidance.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer defines the effects. A 100% answer uses graph language and explains why the net hours effect depends on which effect dominates.

G. Common lost marks: mixing work time and free time; saying MRS is wage; ignoring income effect; treating every feasible point as optimal.

H. Panic version: Wage is the opportunity cost of free time. Optimum: MRS=MRT. Wage rise steepens or rotates the budget line. Substitution effect reduces free time; income effect may increase free time. Hours worked are ambiguous.

I. Active recall version

Question: Explain a wage increase in the labour-leisure model.

Hidden answer: define budget/MRT, define MRS, state MRS=MRT, then separate substitution and income effects and conclude ambiguity.

Unit 4: Nash Equilibrium vs Pareto/Social Optimum 8 marks

A. Question wording: Two firms can each choose Clean or Pollute. If both choose Clean, payoffs are (4,4). If one Pollutes while the other chooses Clean, the polluter gets 6 and the clean firm gets 1. If both Pollute, payoffs are (2,2). Find the Nash equilibrium and compare it with the social optimum.

B. What the examiner is testing: Payoff matrix, best responses, dominant strategy, Nash equilibrium, social optimum and Pareto comparison.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: strategic interaction / social dilemma.
  • Rule: Nash = mutual best response.
  • Draw matrix: Clean/Clean, Pollute/Clean, Clean/Pollute, Pollute/Pollute.
  • Interpret: private incentives can conflict with social welfare.
  • Avoid: comparing one player's payoff with the other player's payoff.
D. Full model answer

A Nash equilibrium is a mutual best response: each player is choosing the best strategy given the other player's strategy. If Firm B chooses Clean, Firm A prefers Pollute because 6 is greater than 4. If Firm B chooses Pollute, Firm A also prefers Pollute because 2 is greater than 1. By symmetry, Pollute is also Firm B's best response whether Firm A chooses Clean or Pollute. Therefore Pollute is a dominant strategy for both firms and the Nash equilibrium is Pollute/Pollute with payoffs (2,2). The social optimum is Clean/Clean because total payoff is 8, compared with 4 at Pollute/Pollute and 7 in the asymmetric outcomes. Clean/Clean is a Pareto improvement over the Nash equilibrium because both firms move from 2 to 4. The game is a social dilemma: individually rational choices lead to an outcome that is worse for both players than cooperation. The conclusion is that Nash equilibrium does not necessarily mean Pareto efficiency or social desirability.

E. Mark allocation: 3 best-response logic; 1 Nash; 1 dominant strategy; 1 social optimum; 1 Pareto comparison; 1 conclusion.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer identifies the Nash and social optimum. A 100% answer shows the best-response method and explains the social dilemma mechanism.

G. Common lost marks: comparing across players; stopping after one cell; saying Nash is fair or efficient; choosing social optimum as Nash.

H. Panic version: Mark best responses. Pollute beats Clean for each firm regardless of the other choice, so Pollute/Pollute is Nash. Clean/Clean gives both 4 instead of 2, so it is socially better and a Pareto improvement over Nash.

I. Active recall version

Question: Find Nash and compare with the social optimum.

Hidden answer: test unilateral deviations, identify Pollute/Pollute as Nash, compare total payoffs and show Clean/Clean is Pareto-superior.

Unit 5: Angela-Bruno, Efficiency, Bargaining Power and Fairness 10 marks

A. Question wording: In an Angela-Bruno allocation, one outcome lies on the feasible frontier and satisfies Angela's reservation indifference curve, but gives Bruno most of the surplus. Explain whether the outcome is efficient, voluntary and fair.

B. What the examiner is testing: Institutions, power, Pareto efficiency, feasible frontier, reservation IC, bargaining, procedural and substantive fairness.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: Unit 5 institutions and allocation.
  • Rule: Pareto efficient means no feasible Pareto improvement remains.
  • Draw: feasible frontier, BSC if relevant, reservation IC and bargaining outcome.
  • Interpret: efficiency, voluntariness and fairness are separate tests.
  • Avoid: saying efficient means fair.
D. Full model answer

Institutions are the rules of the game that determine who can do what and who gets what. In the Angela-Bruno model, the feasible frontier shows technically feasible allocations. An allocation on the feasible frontier can be Pareto efficient because moving along the frontier to make one person better off usually makes the other worse off. If the allocation satisfies Angela's reservation indifference curve, Angela is at least as well off as her reservation option, so the agreement may be voluntary in the narrow economic sense. However, voluntary does not automatically mean fair. If Bruno has the power to make a take-it-or-leave-it offer, he may capture most of the surplus while leaving Angela just on her reservation IC. That outcome can be efficient and voluntary but still substantively unfair because the distribution is unequal and reflects bargaining power. Procedural fairness asks whether the process and rules were fair; substantive fairness asks whether the outcome distribution is fair. The conclusion is that efficiency, voluntariness and fairness must be analysed separately: a high-mark answer should not collapse Pareto efficiency into justice.

E. Mark allocation: 2 model/graph; 2 Pareto efficiency; 2 reservation/voluntary logic; 2 bargaining power; 2 fairness distinction.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer says efficient is not fair. A 100% answer uses the feasible frontier, reservation IC, surplus distribution and procedural/substantive fairness language.

G. Common lost marks: treating BSC as RIC; saying voluntary=fair; using total output only; ignoring bargaining power.

H. Panic version: On the frontier can be Pareto efficient. On Angela's reservation IC can be voluntary. But Bruno capturing most surplus may be unfair. Efficiency, voluntariness and fairness are separate.

I. Active recall version

Question: Is the Angela-Bruno outcome efficient, voluntary and fair?

Hidden answer: test frontier/Pareto efficiency, reservation IC/voluntariness, then bargaining power and procedural/substantive fairness separately.

Unit 2: Technology Choice, Opportunity Cost, Economic Rent and Isocost 8 marks

A. Question wording: A firm can use Technique A with 4 workers and 2 machines or Technique B with 2 workers and 5 machines. Wage is R100 and machine rental is R80. Which technique is cheaper? Explain how opportunity cost and economic rent relate to the firm's decision.

B. What the examiner is testing: Cost equation, isocost logic, relative input prices, opportunity cost, economic rent and technology choice.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: Unit 2 technology choice.
  • Formula: C=wL+rK; economic rent=chosen payoff-next best payoff.
  • Draw: isocost line with slope -w/r if K is vertical and L is horizontal.
  • Interpret: firms choose least-cost technique for the same output.
  • Avoid: choosing by engineering/output only when output is fixed.
D. Full model answer

Technology choice depends on the cost of inputs, not only on the physical technique. The cost equation is C=wL+rK. Technique A costs 100(4)+80(2)=400+160=R560. Technique B costs 100(2)+80(5)=200+400=R600. If both techniques produce the same output, Technique A is cheaper by R40 and is the cost-minimising choice. The opportunity cost of choosing A is the value of the next best alternative, here the forgone option B. If choosing A gives the same revenue but costs R40 less, the firm earns an economic rent of R40 relative to B. On an isocost graph with labour on the horizontal axis and capital/machines on the vertical axis, the slope is -w/r = -100/80 = -1.25, showing the relative price of labour to machines. If wages rose or machines became cheaper, the cost-minimising technique could change. The conclusion is that incentives and relative prices guide technology choice.

E. Mark allocation: 2 cost calculations; 1 choice; 1 isocost slope; 2 opportunity cost/rent; 2 interpretation.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer computes the cheaper technique. A 100% answer links the calculation to isocosts, relative prices and rent.

G. Common lost marks: ignoring one input; reversing isocost slope; calling all payoff rent; forgetting output is held constant.

H. Panic version: A costs R560. B costs R600. Choose A if output is the same. Rent relative to B is R40. Isocost slope is -w/r=-100/80. Relative input prices determine technique choice.

I. Active recall version

Question: Choose the cheaper technique and explain rent.

Hidden answer: compute C=wL+rK for both, choose lower cost, compare chosen payoff to next best alternative and mention isocost slope.

Unit 1: GDP, Capitalism, Prosperity, Inequality and Planetary Limits 8 marks

A. Question wording: A country has rising GDP per capita, but high inequality and worsening environmental damage. Explain why GDP per capita is useful but incomplete as a measure of prosperity, and link this to capitalism and planetary limits.

B. What the examiner is testing: GDP per capita, PPP/real interpretation, wellbeing limits, capitalism, inequality, technological progress, institutions and environmental constraints.

C. 30-second planning box:
  • Identify the model: Unit 1 prosperity and limits.
  • Rule: GDP per capita = GDP/population; it is an average market-output measure.
  • Concepts: capitalism = private property, markets and firms.
  • Interpret: growth can raise material prosperity but hide inequality and environmental costs.
  • Avoid: saying GDP is wellbeing.
D. Full model answer

GDP per capita is useful because it measures average market production per person and can indicate the material resources available in an economy. If adjusted for purchasing power or real prices, it helps compare living standards across time or countries. However, GDP per capita is incomplete as a measure of prosperity. It is an average, so it can rise even if gains go mainly to high-income groups while many people remain poor. It also excludes unpaid household production and does not directly measure health, security, leisure or dignity. Environmental damage can even raise measured GDP if cleanup or defensive spending increases market activity, while the loss of natural systems is not fully counted as a fall in wellbeing. Capitalism, based on private property, markets and firms, can generate innovation and productivity growth, helping explain the hockey-stick rise in output. But market incentives may also ignore planetary limits when environmental costs are externalised. The best conclusion is balanced: GDP per capita is a useful indicator of material output, but an 80%+ answer must discuss distribution, non-market wellbeing, institutions and ecological sustainability.

E. Mark allocation: 2 GDP/per capita usefulness; 2 limitations; 1 capitalism definition; 1 growth mechanism; 1 inequality; 1 planetary limits conclusion.

F. 80% answer vs 100% answer: An 80% answer says GDP is useful but incomplete. A 100% answer adds capitalism, inequality, non-market work and planetary boundaries in one coherent argument.

G. Common lost marks: saying GDP per capita is everyone's income; ignoring inequality; defining capitalism as no government; treating environment as outside economics.

H. Panic version: GDP per capita is average market output per person. It helps compare material living standards, especially with PPP/real adjustment. But it misses inequality, unpaid work, non-market wellbeing and environmental damage. Capitalism can drive growth, but prosperity must respect planetary limits.

I. Active recall version

Question: Explain GDP per capita as useful but incomplete.

Hidden answer: define GDP per capita, explain average market output, then add inequality, unpaid work, non-market wellbeing, capitalism and planetary limits.
